# Structure-first dungeon generation: graphs, locks, and grammars

**Structure-first techniques solve a problem that no spatial method can: they guarantee that a dungeon's progression makes sense before a single tile is placed.** A BSP tree or cellular automaton produces geometry and derives connectivity afterward; a structure-first generator produces a directed graph of tasks, locks, keys, and goals, then hands that graph to a spatial realization layer to become rooms and corridors. This inversion — structure before space — is what Joris Dormans calls the **mission/space duality**: every action-adventure level contains a mission (the logical ordering of player tasks) and a space (the geometric layout), and "the same mission can be mapped to many different spaces, and one space can support multiple different missions" (Dormans, FDG 2010). The three techniques below operate at this structural layer. They assume a spatial realization method from Pass 3a (BSP, room-and-corridor, cellular automata, or room templates) will handle geometry downstream.

---

## 1. Graph-driven dungeon generation

Space-first methods like BSP or room-and-corridor place rooms, then connect them, then hope the resulting connectivity supports interesting gameplay. Graph-driven generation reverses this pipeline entirely: it constructs an abstract topology graph specifying which rooms connect and what relationships they have — hub rooms, dead-end branches, cycles, chokepoints — then feeds that graph to a spatial realization algorithm that assigns positions, shapes, and corridors. The motivation is **topological control**. A designer (or algorithm) who needs a dungeon with exactly two paths from entrance to boss, a central hub with four spokes, and a hidden shortcut loop cannot express that requirement in BSP or cellular automata. A graph encodes it directly. Games requiring Zelda-style item-gated progression, branching narrative paths, or designer-authored topological templates depend on this level of structural control (van der Linden, Lopes & Bidarra, IEEE TCIAIG 2014).

The algorithm has two sharply distinct passes. **Pass 1 — graph construction** produces a labeled graph where nodes represent rooms or areas and edges represent connections (doors, corridors, one-way valves). This graph can be designer-authored, procedurally generated via graph grammars (covered in Section 3), or produced by any method that outputs a planar graph. Node labels encode room type (combat arena, treasure room, hub); edge labels encode connection type (locked door, open passage, one-way drop). **Pass 2 — spatial realization** assigns each node a physical position, shape, and door locations such that (a) no two rooms overlap, (b) every edge in the graph corresponds to a traversable connection, and (c) the result fits within the target map bounds. The most algorithmically detailed implementation of this pass is Nepožitek's Edgar system (Game-ON 2018; Nepožitek & Gemrot). Edgar takes a planar connectivity graph and a set of polygonal room templates with marked door positions as input. It precomputes **configuration spaces** for every pair of room templates — the set of all relative positions where two room polygons do not overlap and share a door-compatible boundary segment. It then decomposes the graph into **chains** (subgraphs where each node has at most two neighbors), prioritizing the smallest faces (cycles) first via BFS because cycles are empirically the hardest constraints to satisfy (Nepožitek, blog series 2018). Each chain is laid out using simulated annealing with an energy function `E = e^(ω·A) · e^(ω·D) − 1`, where A is total room-pair intersection area, D is the sum of squared distances between neighbors not yet in contact, and ω is derived from average room area (Nepožitek & Gemrot, Game-ON 2018). New positions during SA perturbation are chosen using configuration space intersections rather than random placement, dramatically improving convergence. The algorithm stores multiple partial layouts per chain to enable stack-based backtracking: if a later chain cannot be satisfied, the system pops the stack and tries an alternative partial layout for the previous chain.

The main variants differ in how spatial realization works. **Grid embedding** (Unexplored, Dormans 2017) avoids the hard problem of arbitrary graph embedding entirely by constructing the graph on a fixed 5×5 grid from the start — nodes are never moved, only annotated, so spatial realization reduces to resolution doubling and tile expansion (Boris the Brave, 2021). **Force-directed layout** (Dormans & Bakkes, IEEE TCIAIG 2011) treats graph nodes as charged particles with spring-connected edges, iterating Fruchterman-Reingold or Kamada-Kawai forces until equilibrium produces room positions; this gives organic layouts but suffers from local minima on dense graphs. **Configuration-space SA** (Edgar) is the most general: it handles arbitrary planar graphs with heterogeneous room shapes and explicit door positions, at the cost of higher computational overhead and the requirement that the input graph be planar. A fourth variant, geometry-first with graph derivation (TinyKeep/Adonaac, Game Developer 2015), inverts the pipeline — rooms are placed via physics simulation, then Delaunay triangulation + MST extracts a connectivity graph — but this is fundamentally space-first with graph-based connectivity rather than structure-first.

The primary failure modes are **non-planarity conflicts** (if the input graph is non-planar, it cannot be embedded in 2D without crossings, and Edgar's face decomposition algorithm requires planarity), **cycle satisfaction failure** (cycles require rooms to form closed loops, and if room templates are too large or door positions too constrained, the cycle cannot close — this is why Edgar front-loads cycles in chain ordering), and **space exhaustion** (early chains consume space needed by later chains, triggering backtracking). For force-directed layouts, **local minima** produce tangled, overlapping layouts, especially for graphs with more than ~30 nodes; mitigation involves using Kamada-Kawai for initial placement then Fruchterman-Reingold for refinement (Kobourov survey, 2012). Grid-based approaches like Unexplored's avoid most of these failures but constrain maximum connectivity to four orthogonal neighbors per node and cap total room count at roughly the grid size (Boris the Brave, 2021).

Graph-driven generation uses space-first techniques as its realization layer directly. Edgar's room templates are the same polygonal building blocks used in room-template placement systems. Unexplored's final tile-generation pass uses **cellular automata** for cave-shaped rooms and rectangular room-growing algorithms for structured rooms — standard Pass 3a techniques applied per-node after the graph determines topology (Boris the Brave, 2021). Force-directed layouts produce room positions that can feed into any corridor-carving algorithm (L-shaped corridors, A* pathfinding). The graph itself can serve as input to WFC by constraining which tile adjacencies are permitted between rooms of different types. Biome assignment becomes a graph-coloring problem: assign biome labels to graph nodes such that adjacent nodes share compatible biomes or explicit transition zones.

## 2. Lock-and-key graph structures

Space-first dungeons can be fully connected and spatially interesting yet completely lack meaningful progression. A player given access to all rooms simultaneously has no sense of direction, discovery, or mounting challenge. Lock-and-key structures solve this by imposing a **partial ordering on traversal**: certain rooms are inaccessible until the player acquires a specific item, ability, or triggers a specific event elsewhere in the dungeon. This is not decoration applied after layout — it is a structural constraint that determines which regions of the dungeon are reachable at each stage of play. The technique produces dungeons where the critical path, optional branches, backtracking patterns, and difficulty progression are all specified before spatial layout begins. It is essential for Zelda-style dungeons, Metroidvania ability-gating, and any game where progression is paced by item acquisition rather than combat alone.

The core data structure is a **directed graph where nodes are rooms, edges are connections, and a subset of edges carry lock conditions**. Each lock condition references a corresponding key node placed elsewhere in the graph. The fundamental solvability invariant is: **for every lock L requiring key K, the node containing K must be reachable from the start without passing through L.** The simplest implementation that guarantees this invariant is Metazelda's key-level system (Tom Coxon, GitHub, used in *Lenna's Inception*). Metazelda organizes rooms into **key-levels** numbered 0 through N. Key-level 0 contains all rooms reachable from the start without any keys. The key for key-level 1 is placed in the highest-intensity room of key-level 0. Key-level 1 contains rooms reachable once that key is obtained. This continues through all levels. Generation proceeds by growing a spanning tree from the entrance: at each step, a random placed room with an adjacent empty space spawns a child room. At random intervals, the algorithm increments the key-level and places a locked edge, beginning a new key-level. After tree generation, a **graphify phase** adds cycle edges between rooms at the same or compatible key-levels to create loops and reduce linearity — but never adds edges that bypass a lock (Coxon, Metazelda source). A `LinearDungeonGenerator` wrapper generates multiple candidate dungeons, runs A* respecting locked doors on each, and selects the candidate with minimum backtracking (fewest rooms traversed more than once on the shortest path).

An even simpler approach is the tree-based method documented by ShaggyDev (2021). For each new node i, a parent is chosen randomly from existing nodes, and a key for node i's lock is placed at a randomly chosen existing node. Because both parent and key location are chosen from nodes with indices less than i, the key is always reachable before the lock — solvability is guaranteed by construction. This approach generates only trees (no cycles), which means heavy backtracking; mitigations include capping keys per node (`max_keys_per_node`), biasing parent selection toward nodes near the key, and post-hoc addition of shortcut edges (ShaggyDev, 2021). The GraphDungeonGenerator by Ahmed Khalifa (GitHub, amidos2006) takes a grammar-based approach, tracking an **access level** per node — the count of Lock or Puzzle nodes between that node and Start. Its eight grammar rules (including `addKeyLock`, `addLever`, `moveLockBack`) manipulate the graph while maintaining the invariant that keys never appear behind their own locks. The access-level system makes this invariant trivially verifiable after each rule application.

Lock types vary substantially in generation difficulty. **Unique keys** (one key opens one specific lock, e.g., boss key) are the simplest — just ensure the key node is reachable before the lock node. **Persistent keys** (reusable, e.g., Doom colored keys) work similarly but open multiple locks. **Fungible consumable keys** (any small key opens any lock, consumed on use, e.g., Zelda small keys) are dramatically harder: the generator must verify that the player cannot reach a state where they have spent all available keys on wrong doors and are softlocked. Multiple sources explicitly flag this as an unsolved or under-documented generation problem (ShaggyDev, 2021; Boris the Brave, 2021). **Stateful locks** (e.g., water level switches, on/off toggles) require simulating all possible state sequences to verify solvability — virtually no concrete generation guidance exists in the literature (ShaggyDev, 2021). Smith, Padget & Vidler (FDG 2018) model Zelda-style dungeons with three key classes per dungeon — non-particular consumable keys, a particular persistent key item (e.g., hookshot), and a particular consumable boss key — and use Answer Set Programming to constrain valid orderings, but their spatial realization is minimal.

The most common failure modes are **retry exhaustion** (Metazelda throws `RetryException` after 20 failed attempts when spatial constraints are too tight; GraphDungeonGenerator allows 100 retries per component), **excessive backtracking** in the player experience (tree-based generators naturally create deep backtracking unless cycles or shortcuts are added post-hoc), **key clustering** (simple random placement concentrates keys in early nodes, making early rooms feel overloaded and later rooms empty), and **layout impossibility** (a structurally valid lock-and-key graph may not be embeddable in the target spatial format, e.g., a complex branching graph may not fit on a small grid). Calvin Ashmore's foundational thesis (Georgia Tech, 2006) introduced **tension curves** — jagged sawtooth intensity patterns where difficulty rises within each key-level and drops when a new key is found — adopted directly by Metazelda's intensity system to prevent monotonic difficulty.

Lock-and-key generation connects to spatial realization by treating each node as a room to be placed by a space-first method. Metazelda and GraphDungeonGenerator use **grid-based placement** where each graph node occupies one cell with cardinal-direction doors. Unexplored uses progressive grid refinement (5×5 → 10×10 → 50×50 tiles). Runevision's experimental approach (2024) uses Voronoi tessellation — node positions generate Voronoi cells, with walls drawn between cells of different rooms. Any space-first technique from Pass 3a can serve as the realization layer, provided it respects the graph's connectivity and lock placement. The lock-and-key graph also interfaces with WFC: room interiors can be generated by WFC constrained by the room's type label and required door positions. Population passes use the access level or key-level to scale enemy difficulty and loot quality.

## 3. Mission-based and grammar-based layout generation

Graph-driven and lock-and-key techniques require someone — a designer or a higher-level algorithm — to author the structural graph. Grammar-based generation automates that authorship. It uses **formal rewrite rules** to grow a mission graph from a seed, producing arbitrarily complex topologies through repeated rule application. This is the most powerful structure-first technique because it encodes **design principles as rules**: the rule set itself embodies knowledge about what makes a good dungeon (lock before key, rising difficulty, three-act structure, reward after challenge), and every dungeon the grammar produces inherits those properties. The technique is essential when a game needs not just one structural pattern but a **family of structurally valid patterns** that vary each playthrough while maintaining design invariants. Dormans' formulation (FDG 2010; IEEE TCIAIG 2011; PhD thesis 2012) is the foundational work, and his commercial game Unexplored (2017) remains the most ambitious real-world implementation, with approximately **5,000 individual graph rewrite rules** in its dungeon generator and roughly **20,000 rules** in its sequel Unexplored 2 (Boris the Brave, 2021; Protsenko, Dormans & van Rozen, FDG 2025).

Dormans' system operates as a **chain of rewrite systems**, each formalizing a different design principle: "one rewrite system can create a number of tasks, the second might add some dependencies or ensures that the tasks are in an interesting order, the next might add locks and keys to create a non-linear, more space-like mission structure, while another could add bonus tasks and rewards" (Dormans, "Level Design as Model Transformation," 2011). The grammar is not context-free but **context-sensitive** — rules match arbitrary subgraph patterns, not just single symbols — because context-free graph grammars cannot express cycles, which are essential for interesting dungeon topology (Madkour et al., 2021). Generation starts with a **seed graph**: typically a linear chain `Start → T₁ → T₂ → ... → Tₙ → Goal`, where each T is a task node. The chain length determines dungeon size. Rules are then applied according to a **recipe** — an ordered sequence specifying which rules to apply, how many times (exact count or random range), and in what mode. Dormans defines exactly **seven steps** for applying each graph grammar rule (FDG 2010, Section 4): (1) select a group of nodes matching the rule's left-hand side; (2) number the selected nodes according to the left-hand side; (3) remove all edges between the selected nodes; (4) replace numbered nodes with their right-hand-side equivalents (same number); (5) add any right-hand-side nodes without left-hand-side equivalents; (6) add edges as specified by the right-hand side; (7) remove the numbers. Edges not participating in the matched pattern are preserved. Six specific rewrite rules for lock-and-key manipulation are defined in "Level Design as Model Transformation" (2011, Figure 7): Rule 1 converts a task into a lock and branches a key; Rule 2 moves a lock toward the entrance; Rule 3 moves a lock toward the goal (used cautiously — it "breaks with the level design wisdom that it is generally better to have the player encounter the lock before the key"); Rule 4 hides a key by moving a task to precede it; Rule 5 creates multiple keys for a single lock; Rule 6 creates a reusable key via a duplicated terminal lock that cannot be moved by Rule 2.

The main variants differ in grammar formalism and spatial realization strategy. **Graph grammars + shape grammars** (Dormans' original pipeline) use graph grammars for the mission and a separate shape grammar with three primitives — wall, open space, and connection (a non-terminal marker with orientation) — for spatial layout. Each shape rule is associated with a mission terminal, and rules fire in mission-graph order, selecting locations by "relative fitness" (Dormans, FDG 2010 — fitness function is unspecified; this is a documentation gap). **Graph grammars on a fixed grid** (Unexplored) constrain all graph operations to a 5×5 spatial grid, making spatial realization trivial at the cost of topological expressiveness. **Graph grammars + GAN room generation** (Gutierrez & Schrum, 2020) use the grammar for mission backbone and a trained GAN for individual room tile layouts, evaluated as comparably enjoyable to original Zelda dungeons in a 30-player study. **Two-level CFGs + evolutionary optimization** (Font et al., 2016) use one context-free grammar for zone-level topology and another for room-level topology, optimized by evolutionary algorithm for entertainment metrics. **Answer Set Programming** (Smith, Padget & Vidler, FDG 2018) replaces grammar rules with declarative logical constraints solved by Clingo, encoding key-lock ordering, boss placement, and critical path length as hard constraints. L-systems have been used for dungeon passage networks (Antoniuk & Rokita, 2019) but natively generate only trees — cycles require non-standard extensions — limiting their applicability to branching corridor structures rather than full dungeon topology.

Grammar-based generation has three categories of failure modes. **Over-constrained grammars** produce no valid output or only trivially simple outputs when rules are too restrictive; Dormans acknowledges "the construction of generative grammars may be relatively hard and time intensive" (Dormans & Bakkes, IEEE TCIAIG 2011). **Under-constrained grammars** produce degenerate outputs — trivially short levels, "pointless areas" with no gameplay purpose, or levels completable without engaging any challenges — as demonstrated by the basic CFG example where `dungeon → obstacle treasure` can reduce to just `room treasure` (Dormans, FDG 2010). **Spatial realization failure** occurs when a grammatically valid mission graph cannot be physically laid out: the GraphDungeonGenerator uses 100-attempt retry loops per component (Khalifa, GitHub); Unexplored's grid constraint mostly eliminates this at the cost of restricting room count and connectivity. A subtler failure is **predictability**: "the ultimate result of the generation process may initially be difficult to foresee for game developers" (Dormans & Bakkes, 2011), and tuning a grammar requires extensive playtesting because the relationship between individual rules and emergent dungeon properties is indirect. Dormans notes explicitly that grammar design requires "expert game designers or the use of evolutionary algorithms" (FDG 2010); no formal verification method for grammar correctness is provided in any of his papers.

Grammar-based generation's relationship to Pass 3a techniques is the mission/space duality itself: **the grammar produces the structure, and space-first techniques produce the geometry.** Unexplored's pipeline uses cellular automata for cave rooms, rectangular room-growing for structured rooms, and noise-based terrain for floor variation — all standard spatial methods applied within the structure the grammar defines (Boris the Brave, 2021). Shape grammar rules in Dormans' original system produce corridors and rooms from a three-symbol alphabet (wall, open space, connection) that functions as a minimal room-and-corridor generator. Edgar's configuration-space SA can serve as the realization layer for any grammar-produced graph, provided the graph is planar. WFC can generate room interiors constrained by door positions that the grammar determines. The grammar output also drives population passes: Unexplored resolves abstract non-terminals (Obstacle → specific enemy, Lock → specific door type) based on biome and theme labels assigned during graph construction, so the grammar controls not only topology but theming and difficulty distribution.

## How mission graphs encode and realize progression

This section provides additional depth on lock-and-key and mission graph generation, covering the formal representation of progression, the concrete steps of mission-to-space conversion, the state of known implementations, and where documentation is thin.

### Progression constraints as graph properties

A mission graph encodes progression through three mechanisms. **Directed edges** impose a partial ordering: if node A has a directed edge to node B, the player must complete A before B becomes available. **Lock-key pairs** are the primary gating mechanism: a lock node on the critical path blocks progress; a key node on a reachable side branch enables passage through the lock; a special edge (rendered as a dashed line in Dormans' notation) connects each key to its corresponding lock, maintained through all graph manipulations. **Tight vs. loose coupling** (Dormans, FDG 2010) distinguishes edges where the subordinate node "must be placed behind the superordinate in the generated space" (tight, double arrow) from edges where the subordinate "can be placed anywhere" (loose, single arrow). The critical path is the minimum set of nodes and edges from Start to Goal that passes through all mandatory locks. Optional branches are subtrees or subgraphs reachable from the critical path but not required to reach the Goal — they contain treasure, bonus challenges, or alternative routes. Mark Brown's Boss Keys analysis system (YouTube, 2015–2017) provides the definitive visual notation for analyzing these structures in existing Zelda dungeons, distinguishing small keys (fungible, consumable), key items (unique, persistent), boss keys (unique, consumable), and switch-activated barriers. Multiple generators — including GraphDungeonGenerator — explicitly aim to reproduce structures matching Brown's notation (Boris the Brave, 2021).

### The mission-to-space pipeline in Dormans' framework

Dormans describes three distinct spatial realization methods across his publications, each more mature than the last. **Method 1: shape grammar jigsaw** (FDG 2010) processes mission nodes in sequence: for each mission terminal, the algorithm finds associated shape grammar rules, selects one by weight, finds candidate placement locations, selects one by "relative fitness," and applies the rule while storing a reference back to the mission node. After each placement, a reconnection step checks for nearby aligned connections and links them, creating shortcuts and loops. Commands attached to rules can close all open connections before or after placement to prevent short-circuiting the mission. After all mission nodes are placed, the shape grammar continues in normal mode until all non-terminal connections are resolved. **Method 2: force-directed graph layout** (IEEE TCIAIG 2011) simulates mission graph nodes as repulsive particles with spring-connected edges, producing an organic 2D layout. Shape grammar rules then add room geometry and corridor detail. **Method 3: confluent rewrite system** ("Level Design as Model Transformation," 2011) transforms mission nodes into space nodes via a dedicated rewrite system with rules named "create entrance," "place game element," "create lock," "resolve path from game element," and others. This system is "both confluent and terminal: it will always generate the same structure for the same input (although room sizes are set randomly)" (Dormans, 2011). The critical gap Dormans flags explicitly: **"the transformation from space graphs to space maps is currently implemented by an algorithm very specific to the domain of top-down two-dimensional spaces. To my knowledge no out-of-the-box rewrite rules exist that can transform a graph into a map. More research into how this step might be implemented in a more generic way is warranted"** (Dormans, 2011, Section 7).

### Known implementations and what each concretely does

**Unexplored** (Dormans/Ludomotion, 2017) is the most complete implementation. It constructs graphs on a 5×5 grid, draws a circular loop between entrance and goal, selects one of **24 major cycle types** (two-alternative-routes, lock-and-key across arcs, three-lever hub, central lake, foreshadowing, and others), adds minor cycles and dead ends, resolves non-terminal symbols to concrete content based on biome, then expands the grid through three resolution stages: 5×5 → doubled to ~10×10 (inserting corridor tiles) → multiplied 5× to ~50×50 final tile map. Per-room shape rules then apply: doors shrink to single tiles, rooms grow into rectangles with boundary walls, caves and tunnels use small cellular automata. The entire system runs through ~50 PhantomGrammar modules with ~5,000 rules for the dungeon generator; Unexplored 2 uses ~20,000 rules (Boris the Brave, 2021; Protsenko et al., FDG 2025). PhantomGrammar supports three execution modes: Normal (apply rule to one random match), LSystem (apply to every match), and Cellular (find all matches, apply simultaneously). The tool is **not publicly available** — this is a significant barrier to reproduction.

**Metazelda** (Tom Coxon, Java, 291 GitHub stars, used in *Lenna's Inception*) generates a spanning tree by repeatedly choosing a random placed room with an adjacent empty space and spawning a child room. At random intervals it increments the key-level and places a locked edge. After tree construction, a graphify phase adds cycle edges between same-key-level rooms. An intensity system based on Ashmore's tension curves (Georgia Tech, 2006) assigns each room a difficulty value creating a sawtooth pattern. The `LinearDungeonGenerator` generates multiple candidates and selects the one with minimum backtracking via A* analysis. Data structures include `Room` objects with parent/children references, Symbol (key) assignments, and intensity values; `Edge` objects with optional Symbol conditions; and a `Map<Integer, Set<Room>>` for key-level tracking.

**GraphDungeonGenerator** (Ahmed Khalifa, C#, GitHub) implements Dormans' approach with eight named grammar rules (`addNormal`, `addPuzzle`, `addKeyLock`, `addNormalToKey`, `addLever`, `makeLink`, `makeBranching`, `moveLockBack`) controlled by a recipe file. Each node tracks an access level (count of Lock/Puzzle nodes between it and Start). The layout phase places rooms on a 2D grid with cardinal-direction doors.

**Edgar** (Ondřej Nepožitek, C#/.NET and Unity, open source) handles only spatial realization — it takes a designer-authored graph and produces geometry via configuration-space-guided simulated annealing with chain decomposition and backtracking. It achieves over **100× speedup** compared to the original Ma et al. (2014) algorithm (Nepožitek & Gemrot, Game-ON 2018). It does not generate mission graphs; it is a realization engine.

**Runevision's dual-graph system** (Rune Skovbo Johansen, blog, 2024) generates a dependency DAG and spatial graph simultaneously using simple graph rewrite rules, lays out nodes using Sugiyama-style layered drawing with node relaxation, and creates room boundaries via Voronoi tessellation. This is actively in development and documented only through blog posts — promising but incomplete as a reference.

### Where documentation is thin, ambiguous, or missing

Five specific gaps would most impede a developer attempting to implement these techniques:

- **Spatial fitness function**: Dormans' FDG 2010 algorithm selects placement locations by "relative fitness," but neither the fitness function nor its parameters are defined in any of his papers. A developer would need to design this function from scratch.
- **Force-directed layout parameters**: The IEEE TCIAIG 2011 paper mentions force simulation for organic graph layout but provides no spring constants, repulsion forces, damping factors, iteration counts, or pseudocode.
- **Graph-to-map transformation**: Dormans flags this as underspecified in his own 2011 paper. The transformation from abstract space graph to concrete tile map is "implemented by an algorithm very specific to the domain of top-down two-dimensional spaces" with no generalizable method offered.
- **Fungible key generation**: No known algorithm generates dungeons with consumable, non-unique keys (Zelda small keys) and guarantees solvability regardless of the order the player uses them. ShaggyDev (2021) and Boris the Brave (2021) both identify this explicitly as an open problem. Metazelda sidesteps it by using only unique "colored" keys.
- **Grammar authoring methodology**: All Dormans papers present 3–6 example rules, but Unexplored uses ~5,000. No systematic methodology for designing, testing, or debugging a production-scale grammar exists in the literature. Dormans acknowledges this requires "expert game designers or the use of evolutionary algorithms" (FDG 2010), but provides no guidance on the former and does not implement the latter. Boris the Brave concludes that much of Unexplored's variety comes from "simply a ton of different manually coded cases" rather than algorithmic elegance. No pseudocode appears in any of Dormans' papers — algorithms are described entirely in natural language with reference to figures.

## BSP, room-and-corridor, and graph-driven generation compared

BSP and room-and-corridor methods produce spatial layouts with emergent connectivity: the developer controls room size distributions, corridor routing heuristics, and density, but the topological structure — which rooms connect to which, whether cycles exist, how the critical path flows — is a side effect of spatial decisions. The resulting dungeons support exploration-focused gameplay where any room is as good as any other, but they cannot guarantee that the player encounters a key before a lock, traverses areas in a designed order, or experiences a difficulty arc. **Graph-driven generation adds topological control and progression gating at the cost of implementation complexity, authoring effort, and spatial naturalness.** A BSP dungeon can be implemented in an afternoon; a grammar-based mission generator with lock-and-key progression and spatial realization requires designing a rule set, implementing graph rewriting, building or integrating a spatial realization engine, and extensive playtesting to tune the grammar. The output quality difference is categorical: space-first methods produce explorable spaces, structure-first methods produce **designed experiences** that happen to be procedurally varied.

The appropriate choice depends on genre. A traditional roguelike where every floor is interchangeable and the player's goal is survival — Nethack, Brogue, Hades — typically needs only space-first generation. A game where each dungeon tells a spatial story through progression gating — Zelda, Metroidvania, immersive sim — requires structure-first generation, or at minimum a post-hoc pass that layers lock-and-key constraints onto a space-first layout (as the PCGBook notes BSP can do by using the partition tree hierarchy for gating). Hybrid approaches are common: TinyKeep places rooms spatially then extracts a connectivity graph via Delaunay + MST, a space-first method with graph-derived structure that provides loop control without full mission authoring.

## Documentation across Pass 3 is sharply uneven

BSP, room-and-corridor, cellular automata caves, and random walk are **excellently documented**: Bob Nystrom's "Rooms and Mazes" article (journal.stuffwithstuff.com, 2014) provides a complete, runnable implementation with interactive demos; Herbert Wolverson's roguelike tutorial covers 20+ spatial methods with full Rust source code; the PCGBook Chapter 3 (Shaker, Liapis, Togelius et al.) gives algorithmic descriptions with code. By contrast, **mission graph generation, grammar-based layout, and lock-and-key progression are poorly documented at implementation level.** Dormans' papers are the theoretical foundation but contain no pseudocode, leave the spatial fitness function and force-directed parameters unspecified, and flag the graph-to-map transformation as an open problem. The most implementation-useful sources are Metazelda's Java source (291 GitHub stars), GraphDungeonGenerator's C# source (37 GitHub stars), ShaggyDev's GDScript tutorial, and Boris the Brave's reverse-engineering of Unexplored — all community efforts rather than primary literature. Unexplored's ~5,000 rules are proprietary and unavailable. Edgar provides excellent spatial realization documentation but does not generate mission graphs. A developer trying to implement Dormans' full mission/space pipeline would need to combine his papers for the conceptual framework, Metazelda or GraphDungeonGenerator for working lock-and-key code, and Edgar or a custom force-directed layout for spatial realization — no single source covers the complete pipeline at implementation depth.

## Source landscape

**Tutorials and practitioner articles**

Bob Nystrom, "Rooms and Mazes" (journal.stuffwithstuff.com, 2014) — the definitive space-first dungeon implementation article, with interactive animated demos showing each step of rooms-then-mazes-then-connectivity; uniquely valuable for its spanning-tree connector approach and dead-end trimming. Herbert Wolverson, *Roguelike Tutorial in Rust* (bfnightly.bracketproductions.com) — the most comprehensive single tutorial covering 20+ spatial generation methods (BSP, cellular automata, drunkard's walk, DLA, Voronoi, WFC, prefabs, builder chaining); uniquely valuable as the only source that demonstrates combining multiple generators via a trait-based API. ShaggyDev, "An Introduction to Procedural Lock and Key Dungeon Generation" (shaggydev.com, 2021) — the clearest introductory tutorial on lock-and-key generation with full GDScript code; uniquely valuable for its honest assessment of failure modes and the only source that explicitly walks through the simplest possible tree-based implementation. Boris the Brave, "Graph Rewriting for Procedural Level Generation" and "Dungeon Generation in Unexplored" (boristhebrave.com, 2021) — the best practitioner-accessible documentation of grammar-based dungeon generation, reverse-engineering Unexplored's pipeline; uniquely valuable as the only detailed English-language description of how a production grammar-based generator works in practice, including PhantomGrammar's execution modes.

**GDC and conference talks**

Brian Bucklew, "End-to-End Procedural Generation in Caves of Qud" (GDC 2019) — documents a multi-pass constructive pipeline using WFC for interior architecture within pre-partitioned regions; uniquely valuable for demonstrating WFC as a local detail generator within a larger pipeline rather than a standalone level generator, and for documenting WFC failure modes (homogeneity, overfitting, contradictions) encountered in production.

**Academic papers**

Joris Dormans, "Adventures in Level Design" (FDG 2010) — introduces the mission/space duality and the seven-step graph grammar rule application procedure; uniquely valuable as the theoretical foundation for all structure-first dungeon generation. Dormans & Bakkes, "Generating Missions and Spaces for Adaptable Play Experiences" (IEEE TCIAIG, 2011) — extends the framework with force-directed spatial layout, player model adaptation, and shape grammar detail rules; uniquely valuable for the most complete single-paper treatment of the full pipeline. Dormans, "Level Design as Model Transformation" (PCGames 2011) — formalizes six specific lock-and-key rewrite rules and the recipe-based control system; uniquely valuable as the only source defining the concrete rules for lock movement and key hiding. Smith, Padget & Vidler, "Graph-based Generation of Action-Adventure Dungeon Levels using Answer Set Programming" (FDG 2018) — demonstrates declarative constraint solving as an alternative to grammar-based generation; uniquely valuable for modeling Zelda-style three-class key systems. Nepožitek & Gemrot, "Fast Configurable Tile-Based Dungeon Level Generator" (Game-ON 2018) — the most algorithmically detailed graph-to-space realization paper, with configuration spaces, chain decomposition, simulated annealing energy functions, and backtracking; uniquely valuable as the only source providing enough detail to reimplement spatial realization from scratch without consulting code. Gutierrez & Schrum, "GAN Rooms in Generative Graph Grammar Dungeons for The Legend of Zelda" (arXiv, 2020) — combines graph grammar mission backbone with GAN-generated room layouts; uniquely valuable for demonstrating ML-based spatial realization within a grammar-driven pipeline. van der Linden, Lopes & Bidarra, "Procedural Generation of Dungeons" (IEEE TCIAIG, 2014) — the most comprehensive survey of dungeon PCG methods, arguing that controllability is the key unsolved challenge; uniquely valuable for its taxonomy and its identification of where methods fall short. Calvin Ashmore, "Key and Lock Puzzles in Procedural Gameplay" (Georgia Tech thesis, 2006) — foundational formalization of lock-and-key puzzle structures and tension curves; uniquely valuable as the theoretical basis for Metazelda's intensity system.

**Textbooks**

Shaker, Togelius & Nelson (eds.), *Procedural Content Generation in Games* (Springer, 2016; pcgbook.com) — Chapter 3 covers constructive spatial methods (BSP, agent-based, cellular automata) with code at github.com/sentientdesigns/constructive; Chapter 5 covers grammars and L-systems with Dormans as co-author; uniquely valuable as the only textbook treatment that spans both space-first and structure-first approaches in a single volume.

**Repositories**

Tom Coxon, Metazelda (github.com/tcoxon/metazelda, Java, 291 stars) — the most complete open-source lock-and-key dungeon generator; uniquely valuable for its key-level system, intensity curves, graphify phase, and LinearDungeonGenerator. Ahmed Khalifa, GraphDungeonGenerator (github.com/amidos2006/GraphDungeonGenerator, C#, 37 stars) — open-source Dormans-style grammar implementation with eight named rules and a recipe system; uniquely valuable as the only open-source implementation of recipe-controlled graph grammar dungeon generation. Ondřej Nepožitek, Edgar-DotNet and Edgar-Unity (github.com/OndrejNepozitek) — open-source graph-to-space realization engine using configuration-space SA; uniquely valuable as the only production-quality spatial realization library that takes an arbitrary planar graph as input.
