# Region partitioning and biome systems for procedural content generation

**Every procedural world needs structure beyond raw elevation data.** Region partitioning transforms continuous scalar fields into discrete, addressable zones that downstream systems—biome assignment, feature placement, pathfinding—can reason about. Biome and climate systems then consume those zones alongside terrain outputs to produce ecologically coherent landscapes. This document covers six region-partitioning techniques and seven biome/climate techniques at implementation depth, in pipeline order. Each technique description assumes the reader already has heightmaps, noise fields, and scalar compositions in hand and explains precisely how the technique consumes or extends those outputs.

---

## 1. Voronoi partitioning and Delaunay triangulation give maps addressable structure

Voronoi partitioning solves a fundamental problem: continuous heightmaps and noise fields have no inherent notion of "region," yet gameplay systems—biome assignment, political boundaries, quest placement—need discrete, addressable polygons with well-defined adjacency. A Voronoi diagram takes a set of seed points and partitions the plane so that every location belongs to the polygon of its nearest seed. The dual of this diagram, the Delaunay triangulation, connects seeds whose Voronoi cells share an edge, producing an adjacency graph for free. Amit Patel's polygon map generator maintains both graphs simultaneously: "The map contains two related graphs. The first graph has nodes for each polygon and edges between adjacent polygons. It represents the Delaunay triangulation… The second graph has nodes for each polygon corner and edges between corners. It contains the shapes of the polygons" (Patel, "Polygonal Map Generation for Games," 2010, updated 2025). This dual-graph architecture is the reason Voronoi dominates over simpler grid-based partitioning for organic maps—it provides both spatial regions and a connectivity graph in a single construction.

The standard implementation pipeline begins with seed point generation. Purely random points produce unacceptably "clumpy" distributions—Patel notes "random numbers are more 'clumpy' than what people expect" (ibid.)—so most implementations use either a **jittered grid** (offset each grid point by a random displacement scaled by a jitter parameter) or **Poisson disc sampling** (which guarantees a minimum distance between points). Patel's mapgen2 uses Poisson disc sampling with a spacing parameter that controls polygon density (mapgen2 GitHub README). Given seed points, the Delaunay triangulation is computed first—typically via the **Delaunator** library in JavaScript or the **Bowyer-Watson** incremental algorithm elsewhere—and the Voronoi diagram is derived as its dual. Bowyer-Watson operates by maintaining a valid triangulation and inserting points one at a time: for each new point, find all triangles whose circumcircle contains it ("bad triangles"), remove them, and re-triangulate the resulting polygonal hole by connecting each boundary edge to the new point (Bowyer, 1981; Watson, 1981). Average-case complexity is **O(n log n)**, though worst-case degenerates to O(n²). Fortune's sweep-line algorithm achieves guaranteed O(n log n) by maintaining a "beach line" of parabolic arcs and processing site events and circle events in sweep order (Fortune, 1986). In practice, Delaunator—a highly optimized half-edge-based Delaunay implementation—is the dominant choice for JavaScript/TypeScript PCG projects due to its speed and the ease of deriving Voronoi cells from its half-edge data structure.

Voronoi cells are constructed from the half-edge structure by walking edges around each point. The Delaunator guide provides the core traversal: starting from an incoming half-edge, follow `nextHalfedge` then look up the opposite half-edge in `halfedges[]`, repeating until returning to the start (Patel, "Delaunator Guide"). Each half-edge in the Delaunay mesh corresponds to one Voronoi edge connecting the circumcenters of two adjacent triangles. A critical failure mode is that **circumcenters can fall outside their triangles**, producing Voronoi edges of zero or near-zero length. Patel documents this: "The blue points might not be inside the Delaunay triangles… the blue points are sometimes right on top of each other. When the blue points are close to each other, the Voronoi edges are too short" ("Alternatives to Voronoi Layouts," 2017). The fix is to use **triangle centroids** instead of circumcenters, producing a "barycentric dual mesh" where the interior points are always inside their triangles and edges are more uniformly spaced. Patel notes that centroids "are spaced evenly" and "the white edges and black edges always cross" (ibid.), though the resulting polygons sacrifice perpendicularity between dual edges and may occasionally be non-convex. A second common failure occurs at **map boundaries**: points on the convex hull have incomplete Voronoi cells extending to infinity. Solutions include clipping to bounds, adding sentinel points outside the map rectangle, or wrapping the mesh with "ghost" half-edges as Patel's dual-mesh library does (mapgen2 GitHub README).

Voronoi partitioning receives seed point positions as input (which may be derived from any spatial distribution strategy) and optionally consumes a heightmap to weight seed density. Its primary outputs are a set of polygonal regions with known centroids, a half-edge mesh for geometric operations, and—via the Delaunay dual—an adjacency graph where each edge connects neighboring polygon centers. These outputs feed directly into elevation assignment (elevation can be stored per-polygon or per-corner), moisture simulation (wind-based algorithms traverse the adjacency graph), biome assignment (each polygon receives a biome label), and river generation (rivers flow along corner-to-corner edges of the Delaunay mesh). In Patel's pipeline, elevation is set on corner points rather than polygon centers because "corner-to-corner edges can serve as ridges and valleys. Rivers flow through corner-to-corner edges" (Patel, 2010). Polygon elevation is then the average of its corners' elevations. For downstream placement systems, the polygon graph provides the spatial index: each polygon's biome, elevation, moisture, and adjacency information is available as an attribute lookup by polygon index.

## 2. Lloyd's relaxation transforms irregular cells into organic, uniform regions

Raw Voronoi diagrams from random or jittered seeds produce polygons that vary wildly in size and shape. Lloyd's relaxation corrects this by iteratively moving each seed to the centroid of its Voronoi cell and recomputing the diagram, converging toward a **centroidal Voronoi tessellation** (CVT) where each seed sits at its cell's center of mass. The algorithm was devised by Stuart P. Lloyd at Bell Labs in 1957 but not published until 1982 ("Least squares quantization in PCM," IEEE Trans. Information Theory, 28(2):129–137). Each iteration requires a full Voronoi recomputation, making the cost per iteration O(n log n) for n seeds. Patel reports that **two iterations** suffice for map generation: "Running it twice gives me good results" and his source code confirms `NUM_LLOYD_RELAXATIONS = 2` (Patel, 2010; Map.as source). More iterations produce increasingly regular hexagonal-like tilings—useful for strategy game maps but potentially too uniform for natural landscapes.

The implementation is straightforward: after computing the Voronoi diagram, calculate the centroid of each polygon (either as the true area-weighted centroid or, as Patel simplifies, the average of corner positions—"instead of computing the centroid I just average the vertices… and get good results," Patel, blog post part 1, 2010), move each seed to its centroid, and recompute. The primary failure mode is **over-relaxation**: too many iterations eliminate the natural size variation that makes maps feel organic. A subtler problem is that Lloyd's relaxation improves cell sizes but does not fix **short edges**. Patel added a separate corner adjustment step: "Just as Lloyd relaxation improves the polygon sizes, I needed to adjust the edge lengths. The adjustment does not preserve Voronoi properties" (Map.as source comments). Short edges cause artifacts in river rendering and road placement because the geometric features placed along edges become compressed below visible thresholds.

Modern implementations often skip Lloyd's relaxation entirely by using **Poisson disc sampling** for seed generation, which produces well-spaced points from the start. Patel confirms this shift: "In newer projects I use a Poisson Disc algorithm, which generates a nice distribution of points from the start, without iterating through multiple steps of Lloyd relaxation" (Patel, 2010 article, updated). Lloyd's relaxation receives a set of Voronoi polygons and their seeds; it outputs a refined set of seeds that, when re-triangulated, produce more uniform polygons. This directly improves all downstream systems: more consistent polygon sizes mean more predictable biome region areas, more uniform river segment lengths, and more evenly distributed placement opportunities for object scattering in later pipeline stages.

## 3. BSP partitions space into non-overlapping convex regions without requiring point seeds

Binary space partitioning (BSP) recursively subdivides a rectangular (or arbitrary convex) region along splitting planes, producing a binary tree whose leaves are non-overlapping convex subregions that tile the original space. Unlike Voronoi, BSP requires no seed points—it operates on the region geometry itself. While BSP is widely associated with dungeon generation, its value as a general spatial partitioning tool is distinct: it creates hierarchical, nested regions with explicit parent-child relationships and guaranteed convexity. The original BSP algorithm was introduced by Fuchs, Kedem, and Naylor for visible surface determination (SIGGRAPH 1980), but in PCG contexts the typical application is axis-aligned recursive subdivision for world-region partitioning, city-block generation, or adaptive detail allocation.

The algorithm takes a bounding rectangle and recursion parameters (maximum depth, minimum area) as input. At each step, choose a split axis (horizontal or vertical—commonly alternating based on which dimension is longer to avoid thin slivers) and a split position along that axis. Random positions between 0.1 and 0.9 of the dimension produce heterogeneous regions; positions between 0.45 and 0.55 produce more balanced, homogeneous regions (RogueBasin, "Basic BSP Dungeon generation"). The region is divided into two child rectangles, and the process recurses on each until a termination condition is met. The tree structure naturally encodes adjacency: sibling leaves share a boundary edge along their parent's split line, making it trivial to build an adjacency graph from the tree. The key failure mode is **thin slivers**—long, narrow regions produced by unfortunate split positions. The standard fix is to enforce a minimum aspect ratio (reject any split producing a child region whose shorter dimension divided by longer dimension falls below ~0.3) and to prefer splitting along the longer axis.

BSP receives a bounding region (which may be the full map extent or a subregion from a higher-level partitioning scheme) and produces a tree of convex subregions. Each leaf can be assigned a biome, terrain style, or generation ruleset. The tree structure feeds into adjacency computation (sibling analysis) and can be combined with Voronoi: BSP provides coarse macro-regions while Voronoi subdivides within each BSP leaf for finer-grained polygon partitioning. For downstream placement, BSP's hierarchical nature supports level-of-detail queries—higher tree nodes represent larger spatial extents, useful for culling or distant rendering.

## 4. Quadtrees and octrees provide adaptive spatial resolution

Quadtrees recursively subdivide 2D space into four equal quadrants, while octrees extend this to 3D with eight octants. Unlike BSP's arbitrary splits, quad/octree divisions are always axis-aligned and centered, creating a regular hierarchy. Their primary value in PCG is **adaptive resolution**: subdivide densely where detail matters (near terrain features, coastlines, or the camera) and leave large uniform areas as single coarse nodes. Thatcher Ulrich's influential article on continuous LOD terrain meshing demonstrated quadtree-based terrain where distant regions use coarse mesh patches and nearby regions use fine patches, with terrain detail generated procedurally on demand ("Continuous LOD Terrain Meshing Using Adaptive Quadtrees," Game Developer/Gamasutra).

A quadtree node stores a bounding rectangle and either data (leaf) or four child nodes. Insertion checks whether the entity falls within the node's bounds; if the node is a leaf and its bucket exceeds capacity (typically 4–16 entities), it subdivides into four children and redistributes. The termination criteria for PCG subdivision are typically terrain variance (subdivide if elevation range within the node exceeds a threshold) or distance to a feature of interest. **Octrees** follow the same logic in 3D—each node splits into eight children. For voxel terrain (caves, overhangs, destructible worlds), **sparse voxel octrees (SVOs)** store only nodes containing surface data, with uniform all-air or all-solid volumes represented as single leaves. This exploits "subvolume uniformity" for dramatic memory savings—a naive dense grid for a planetary-scale world would require trillions of voxels, but an SVO stores only the surface shell (Matt Fichman, "Sparse Voxel Octrees" dev blog). A hybrid approach stores small voxel grids (e.g., 16³) at octree leaves, combining SVO's memory efficiency with greedy meshing's vertex efficiency.

The principal failure mode for terrain quadtrees is **T-junction cracks**: when adjacent nodes differ by more than one LOD level, mesh edges don't align, producing visible seams. The standard fix is the **restricted quadtree** constraint—no two adjacent nodes may differ by more than one subdivision level—combined with stitching triangles at LOD boundaries. For octrees, **LOD transition seams** between different depths require dual contouring or transition cells. Quadtrees and octrees receive a spatial extent and a set of entities or a scalar field (heightmap, density field) to partition. They produce a spatial index that supports O(log n) range queries and nearest-neighbor lookups. In a PCG pipeline, quadtrees are used for spatial queries during object placement (finding nearby entities for spacing constraints), terrain LOD management, and adaptive biome-detail generation. They do not typically define biome boundaries themselves but serve as the spatial acceleration structure that makes region-based queries efficient at runtime.

## 5. Region graphs encode adjacency for constraint-based generation

A region adjacency graph (RAG) represents spatial regions as nodes and shared boundaries as edges, providing the data structure that constraint-based systems—biome assignment, political borders, quest routing—operate on. The most natural source of a RAG is the Voronoi-Delaunay dual: computing the Delaunay triangulation of polygon seeds produces the adjacency graph of Voronoi regions automatically, because two Voronoi cells share an edge if and only if their seeds are connected by a Delaunay edge. Patel's data structure makes this explicit with three interconnected classes: `Center` (polygon) stores lists of `neighbors`, `borders`, and `corners`; `Corner` (Voronoi vertex) stores `touches` (adjacent Centers), `protrudes` (incident Edges), and `adjacent` (neighboring Corners); and `Edge` stores four references—two polygon Centers (d0, d1) and two Corners (v0, v1) (Patel, "Polygonal Map Generation for Games," 2010). This four-reference edge representation allows traversal of both the polygon graph and the corner graph through a single unified structure.

For non-Voronoi partitions (BSP leaves, grid-based regions, painted zones), the RAG must be constructed explicitly. The standard approach for grid-based label maps scans each cell and its right and bottom neighbors; when labels differ, an edge is added between those two region nodes (or an existing edge's weight, representing shared border length, is incremented). For BSP partitions, the tree structure encodes adjacency implicitly—sibling leaves share the parent's split edge—but non-sibling adjacency requires comparing leaf bounding rectangles for shared boundaries. The failure mode for RAG construction is **missing or spurious adjacencies** when regions touch only at a single point (corner-adjacency versus edge-adjacency). Most PCG systems use edge-adjacency (regions must share a boundary segment of nonzero length) to avoid the ambiguities of corner contact.

Region graphs receive the geometric output of any partitioning scheme (Voronoi polygons, BSP leaves, quadtree leaves, painted regions) and produce a graph structure that all downstream constraint-based systems consume. Biome assignment traverses the graph to enforce diversity rules (e.g., no two adjacent polygons share the same rare biome). River generation follows downhill paths through the graph. Moisture simulation in mapgen4 propagates humidity along adjacency edges sorted by wind direction (Patel, mapgen4 blog). Political region generation uses graph coloring. For placement pipelines, the adjacency graph determines which regions' placement rules must be coordinated at shared boundaries to avoid visible discontinuities—a forest-to-desert boundary needs transition vegetation on both sides, which requires knowing that the two regions are adjacent.

## 6. Connected component analysis identifies contiguous landmasses and enclosed bodies

After partitioning and labeling, many generation steps produce spatially fragmented results—small disconnected islands, interior lakes, isolated cave chambers—that need to be identified and either kept, merged, or removed. Connected component analysis (CCA) solves this by labeling maximal groups of contiguous cells or regions sharing a property. The two dominant implementations are **flood fill** (BFS/DFS from each unlabeled foreground cell, marking all reachable cells with the same label) and the **two-pass union-find** algorithm (scan in raster order, assign provisional labels, record equivalences when differently-labeled neighbors are found, then resolve equivalences via a disjoint-set union structure in a second pass). Flood fill is simpler and preferred when only specific seed points need labeling (e.g., "flood fill from the map border through water tiles to mark ocean; any remaining water components are lakes"—this is exactly Patel's approach in his polygon map generator, Patel, 2010). Union-find is preferred for batch labeling all components simultaneously, with near-linear complexity: O(m · α(n)) where α is the inverse Ackermann function (Tarjan, 1975).

The algorithm's key decision is **connectivity type**: 4-connected (cardinal neighbors only) versus 8-connected (including diagonals). The choice affects results—4-connectivity treats diagonally-touching cells as disconnected, which can split a region that 8-connectivity would unify. For polygon-based maps (Voronoi), connectivity is defined by the adjacency graph rather than grid neighbors. The most common failure mode is **noise sensitivity**: noisy generation produces many tiny 1–2 cell components that clutter the label map. The fix is to filter by minimum component size after labeling, removing or merging components below a threshold. A second failure mode specific to recursive flood fill is **stack overflow** on large grids; the fix is to use an explicit queue (BFS) or iterative stack instead of recursion.

CCA receives a labeled grid or a set of regions with binary or categorical labels (land/water, biome type, cave/solid) and produces a component ID for each cell plus component metadata (area, bounding box, centroid). Patel uses it to distinguish ocean from lakes: flood fill from border polygons through water identifies ocean; remaining water polygons are lakes (Patel, 2010). Cave generators use CCA to identify disconnected chambers after cellular automata, then connect the largest components with corridors. For the biome pipeline, CCA validates that all land is reachable (a single connected continent) or identifies distinct landmasses that may receive independent biome assignments. Its outputs—component IDs and sizes—feed into both biome assignment (per-landmass climate simulation) and placement systems (ensuring navigability between placed content).

## 7. The temperature-moisture model maps two scalar fields to biome types via a Whittaker diagram

The Whittaker biome classification, introduced by ecologist Robert Whittaker in 1975 (*Communities and Ecosystems*), maps biomes based on two axes: mean annual temperature and mean annual precipitation. For PCG, this two-axis lookup provides a principled, ecologically grounded method for assigning biomes that produces more plausible results than arbitrary noise thresholds. Patel notes the key insight: "With either biomes or gradients, one noise value doesn't produce enough diversity, but two is pretty good. Two is what ecologist Robert Whittaker used when he was studying biomes" (Patel, "Making maps with noise functions"). The practical implementation converts the triangular Whittaker diagram into a **rectangular 2D lookup table** indexed by discretized temperature and moisture levels. Patel's implementation uses elevation as a temperature proxy (higher elevation = colder) with moisture on the second axis, producing 4 elevation zones × 6 moisture levels mapping to 13 biome types including SNOW, TUNDRA, BARE, SCORCHED, TAIGA, SHRUBLAND, TEMPERATE_DESERT, TEMPERATE_RAIN_FOREST, TEMPERATE_DECIDUOUS_FOREST, GRASSLAND, TROPICAL_RAIN_FOREST, TROPICAL_SEASONAL_FOREST, and SUBTROPICAL_DESERT (Patel, 2010).

The lookup function is a cascade of threshold checks. Patel's `biome(e, m)` function from the terrain-from-noise article checks elevation thresholds first (ocean below 0.1, beach below 0.12, then four elevation bands), and within each band checks moisture thresholds to select a biome. Jon Gallant's Unity implementation uses a 6×6 `BiomeType` array mapping six temperature levels (COLDEST through HOTTEST) against six moisture levels (DRIEST through WETTEST), yielding biomes from Ice and Tundra to Tropical Rainforest ("Procedurally Generating Wrapping World Maps in Unity C#," Game Developer). Azgaar's Fantasy Map Generator uses a finer **100×10 matrix** (100 temperature levels × 10 precipitation levels) supporting approximately 20 biomes for smoother color interpolation (Azgaar, "Biomes Generation and Rendering," 2017). The primary failure mode is the **missing triangle constraint**: the real Whittaker diagram is roughly triangular (very cold + very wet does not naturally pair with very hot + very wet), but rectangular lookup tables permit unrealistic combinations unless the table entries are manually constrained. A second failure mode is **biome fragmentation** when temperature and moisture noise operate at similar high frequencies, producing implausible microbiomes—small patches of desert surrounded by rainforest.

The temperature-moisture model receives two scalar fields—temperature (derived from latitude, elevation via lapse rate, and optional noise) and moisture (from noise, distance-from-water, or wind simulation)—and produces a categorical biome label per region or per pixel. Temperature is typically computed as `base_temp - (latitude_factor) - (elevation × lapse_rate) + noise_perturbation`. The biome labels feed into terrain material selection, vegetation density parameters, creature spawn tables, and biome transition blending. For placement pipelines, the Whittaker biome label determines which asset palette is active for each region.

## 8. Altitude-based biome assignment uses elevation as the sole or primary classification axis

The simplest biome assignment strategy maps elevation directly to biome type through threshold bands: water below a sea-level cutoff, beach in a narrow coastal band, lowland forest, highland shrub, alpine tundra, and snow peaks at the top. Patel demonstrates this in the terrain-from-noise article with a direct elevation-to-biome function: ocean below 0.1, beach below 0.2, forest, jungle, savannah, desert, and snow at increasing thresholds (Patel, "Making maps with noise functions"). This approach works because altitude correlates strongly with real-world ecological zonation—the environmental lapse rate of approximately **6.5°C per 1,000 meters** of elevation gain means mountaintops are dramatically colder than adjacent lowlands regardless of latitude. Azgaar uses this directly: `temperature = coastal_temperature - (cell_height - sea_level) × 20` (Azgaar, 2017).

The key extension is Patel's "equivalent elevation" formula, which folds latitude into the elevation value: `equivalent_elevation = 10*e*e + poles + (equator - poles) * sin(PI * (y / height))`, making polar regions behave like high-altitude regions even at sea level (Patel, "Making maps with noise functions"). This single-axis approach, while fast and easy to implement, lacks the ecological richness of the two-axis Whittaker model—it cannot distinguish between a cold dry tundra and a cold wet boreal forest at the same elevation. Its failure mode is monotony: all points at the same elevation receive identical biomes regardless of moisture conditions, producing unrealistic horizontal bands on large maps. The fix is to add a second axis (moisture or rainfall), upgrading to the full Whittaker lookup. Altitude-based assignment receives a heightmap and optionally a latitude field; it outputs biome labels that feed into the same downstream systems as the Whittaker model but with less ecological diversity.

## 9. Rainfall and rainshadow approximation creates moisture gradients from terrain and wind direction

Real precipitation patterns are dominated by the interaction between prevailing winds and terrain elevation. When moisture-laden air hits a mountain range, it rises, cools, and dumps precipitation on the windward slope. The leeward side receives dry descending air, creating a "rain shadow" desert. Simulating this in PCG produces dramatically more convincing moisture distributions than independent noise—rivers can flow through deserts (fed by distant mountain rainfall) and coastal regions can be dry if they're on the wrong side of a mountain range. Tarn Adams identified rain shadows as a transformative addition to Dwarf Fortress: terrain height affects rainfall so that "moist air from ocean hits mountain, rains on seaward side, dry on other side" (Dwarf Fortress Wiki; Adams, Game AI Pro 2, Chapter 41).

The simplest approximation is a **ray-cast model**: define a prevailing wind direction, then for each row or column along that direction, start with moisture = 1.0 at the ocean edge. As the ray traverses land cells, moisture decreases with distance and drops sharply when elevation increases (orographic precipitation). The moisture remaining after crossing a mountain range is reduced, producing a dry zone on the leeward side. Patel's early work used this approach: "I added a wind algorithm that spreads moisture from west to east… the dry areas in the northwest are now green, and the southeast, where the moisture is blocked by mountains, is dry" (Patel, simblob.blogspot.com, 2010). Fischer et al.'s AutoBiomes paper (2020, *The Visual Computer*) uses a more sophisticated two-step model: wind distributes moisture from cell to cell (primarily in the wind direction, with some lateral diffusion), and precipitation is computed at each step based on the temperature difference between source and target cells—air cooling as it rises forces moisture out. Nick McDonald's procedural weather system goes further, simulating coupled temperature-humidity differential equations over 365 timesteps to produce annual average climate maps (McDonald, "Procedural Weather Patterns," 2018).

The failure modes of rainshadow approximation include **single-direction bias** (a fixed wind direction produces unrealistically symmetric moisture patterns) and **binary mountain effect** (mountains either completely block moisture or don't, with no intermediate behavior). The single-direction fix is to blend results from multiple wind angles or oscillate wind direction to simulate seasons (unconfirmed: suggested in Patel's mapgen4 alternate renderer notes, 2025). Rainfall approximation receives a heightmap and a wind direction vector (or set of vectors); it outputs a moisture/rainfall field per cell. This moisture field feeds directly into the Whittaker biome lookup as the second axis and into river generation as the precipitation source for flow accumulation.

## 10. Wind-based moisture simulation in mapgen4 produces rainshadow from first principles

Patel's mapgen4 implements the most algorithmically detailed publicly documented wind-moisture simulation in the PCG space, going beyond simple ray-casting to simulate evaporation, wind transport, orographic cap, and precipitation as distinct steps per region. The conceptual model is: "Evaporation moves moisture from lakes and oceans into the air. Wind moves the air and moisture. Air can't hold as much moisture when it passes over mountains. When air can't hold the moisture it falls as rain" (Patel, "mapgen4 rainfall," simblob.blogspot.com, 2018). Critically, Patel chose a **unidirectional wind model** over fluid simulation—"I gave up on fluid simulation… I decided I should stick with the decision to skip fluid simulation" (ibid.)—because unidirectional wind allows a single-pass computation with no iteration.

The algorithm's key insight is **topological sorting by wind direction**. Each Voronoi region is assigned a sort value equal to the dot product of its position with the wind direction vector: `wind_sort_r[r] = mesh.x_of_r(r) * windAngleVec[0] + mesh.y_of_r(r) * windAngleVec[1]` (mapgen4, map.ts). Regions are sorted from upwind to downwind. Because wind flows in one direction, region J's humidity depends only on upwind region K—once K is computed, J can be computed, and J never affects K. This eliminates cycles and enables single-pass O(n log n) processing. Patel explicitly notes this advantage: "I can determine ahead of time that cell J depends on cell K… cell J never affects K" (ibid.).

Processing each region in wind order involves four steps. First, **wind transport**: humidity is copied from neighboring upwind regions (averaged over neighbors that are upwind of the current region). Second, **evaporation**: over water bodies (oceans and lakes), humidity increases by the `evaporation` parameter. Third, **orographic cap**: humidity is limited to `1 - elevation`, modeling the physical fact that air at higher altitudes holds less moisture. Fourth, **orographic precipitation**: any humidity exceeding the cap falls as rain. The core formula from map.ts is: `orographicRainfall = biomesParam.rain_shadow * (humidity - (1.0 - elevation_r[r])); rainfall += biomesParam.raininess * orographicRainfall` (mapgen4, map.ts, lines ~242–290, via DeepWiki analysis). The rainshadow is an **emergent property**: when wind carries moisture over a mountain range, the orographic cap forces moisture out on the windward slope; on the leeward side, humidity has already been depleted, producing a dry zone without any explicit rainshadow logic. Four parameters control the system: `wind_angle_deg` (prevailing wind direction), `raininess` (overall rainfall multiplier), `evaporation` (rate of moisture gain over water), and `rain_shadow` (strength of orographic forcing).

The primary failure mode is the **single wind direction assumption**, which produces unrealistic symmetry—real climates involve trade winds, westerlies, and monsoon oscillation. Patel acknowledged this limitation and suggested oscillating wind direction to simulate seasons (mapgen4 alternate renderer notes, 2025). A secondary failure mode is **moisture starvation** in large interior continents: if the map is wide and mountainous, all moisture may be deposited near the coast, leaving the interior uniformly dry regardless of local topology. Increasing evaporation from lakes and rivers partially mitigates this. The simulation receives the Voronoi mesh with per-region elevation values (from the elevation assignment step) and a set of water-body flags; it outputs `rainfall_r` (precipitation per region) and `humidity_r` (atmospheric moisture per region). These feed into biome assignment as the moisture axis of the Whittaker lookup and into river generation as the precipitation source: regions with high rainfall contribute more flow to the downslope accumulation system. Patel notes the crucial difference from mapgen2's moisture model: "Those biomes were based on the distance to the rivers. That means you could never have a river going through a desert. Here, rainfall and river flow are related, but it can rain in a wet place and cause a river to flow through a dry place" (ibid.).

## 11. Biome masks and noise-based selection use layered noise to control biome distribution

When a full climate simulation is unnecessary or too expensive, biome masks provide a lightweight alternative: multiple independent noise layers serve as masks that filter which biomes can appear at each location. The standard approach uses a height noise layer (land/water), a temperature noise layer (cold/hot), and a moisture noise layer (dry/wet), with each layer acting as a binary or gradient mask. Thresholds on each layer narrow the set of eligible biomes until a single selection remains. The Cuberite voxel engine documentation traces the evolution of this approach: from a simple Voronoi cell generator (each cell = one biome, biome chosen by hash of seed position) to a distorted Voronoi generator (Perlin noise warps lookup coordinates for organic borders) to a temperature-humidity model where "two separate Perlin noise fields define temperature and humidity. Biome is selected from a 2D lookup table indexed by [temperature][humidity]" (Cuberite Generator documentation, cuberite.xoft.cz). The temperature-humidity noise approach "elegantly prevents impossible adjacencies (desert next to tundra) without explicit neighbor constraints" because adjacent points in noise space map to adjacent points in the Whittaker table (ibid.).

Minecraft's post-1.18 multi-noise system extends this to **six noise parameters**: temperature, humidity, continentalness, erosion, weirdness, and depth. Each biome is defined as a 6D interval in this parameter space. At each location, all six noise values are sampled and the biome whose defined interval is closest in **6D Euclidean distance** is selected (minecraft.wiki, "World generation"). Temperature and humidity use five discrete levels each, continentalness controls ocean/inland placement, erosion (seven levels) controls flat versus mountainous terrain, and weirdness drives biome variants. Resolution is one biome sample per 4×4×4 block section. This multi-noise approach prevents fragmentation by using noise frequencies that are **4× slower than terrain noise** for the biome-controlling parameters (Dawnosaur Substack; Alan Zucconi, "Minecraft World Generation," 2022). A key failure mode of noise-based selection is **microbiome fragmentation**—if biome-controlling noise operates at too high a frequency, the landscape becomes a confetti of tiny biome patches. The fix is to ensure biome noise wavelengths are significantly larger than terrain noise wavelengths.

Biome masks receive the heightmap and any number of additional noise fields; they output a biome label or a set of biome weights per cell. A common pattern is to generate base biomes from temperature-moisture masks, then overlay additional noise maps that **override** with special biomes only where the override noise exceeds a threshold. This layered approach allows rare biomes (mushroom islands, crystal caves) to appear at controlled densities without disrupting the base climate model. The outputs feed into terrain material selection, transition blending, and placement systems.

## 12. Biome transition blending eliminates hard boundaries between adjacent biome regions

Hard biome boundaries produce jarring visual artifacts—abrupt color changes, discontinuous terrain heights, and vegetation lines that look like map borders rather than ecological transitions. Blending creates smooth weight gradients across boundary zones where multiple biomes contribute proportionally, with `sum(weights) = 1.0` at every point. Several methods exist with different performance-quality tradeoffs. **Grid-based interpolation** (Minecraft's approach) samples biomes on a coarse grid and linearly interpolates between grid points. This is fast but produces "visible grid-aligned boundaries and regularly-spaced creases" (noiseposti.ng, "Fast Biome Blending Without Squareness," 2021). **Gaussian blur** applies a blur kernel to a full-resolution biome map, with a weight function such as `max(0, radius² - dx² - dy²)²`; this produces smooth transitions but is expensive for large blend radii (KdotJPG, Simple-Biome-Blending, GitHub).

The best-documented tradeoff is the **scattered blend** approach: replace the regular sampling grid with a jittered triangular or hexagonal point distribution (similar to Voronoi noise) and use **normalized sparse convolution** over nearby data points with a smooth falloff kernel. This eliminates grid artifacts while maintaining reasonable performance. At each point, nearby scattered samples contribute biome weights based on distance through the kernel function, and weights are normalized to sum to 1.0. The output at a transition zone might be `{Forest: 0.6, Plains: 0.4}`, with these weights changing gradually across the boundary (noiseposti.ng, 2021). The PSWG (Galaxies) mod uses a **multi-composite terrain** approach where a master Simplex noise value `l` determines interpolation between biome-specific heightmaps: `weight_i = max(0, -|n·l - i| + 1)` where `n` is the number of layers and `i` is the layer index. Weights always sum to 1.0 by construction, and the final terrain height is the weighted sum of per-biome heightmaps (parzivail.com, "Procedural Terrain Generation"). A fourth approach adds **Perlin noise to boundary coordinates**, distorting the boundary line itself for an organic, irregular transition rather than a smooth gradient.

The critical principle is continuity: "The most important thing to remember is that everything must be continuous; if any discrete event happens during the events above, it will look bad" (Emre Safa Baltacı, Medium). The primary failure mode is **weight discontinuities** at blend boundaries—if the blend radius is too small or the kernel function has a discontinuous derivative, visible seams appear. The fix is to use smooth (C¹ or C² continuous) falloff functions and ensure blend radii are wide enough to be visually invisible. Biome transition blending receives per-region biome labels and the geometric mesh; it outputs per-point biome weight maps. These weights control terrain height blending (each biome contributes its heightmap proportional to its weight), material/texture blending, vegetation density interpolation, and any other biome-dependent parameter. For placement pipelines, transition zones receive reduced placement density from each contributing biome rather than full density from either.

## 13. Local versus global biome assignment trade designer control against deterministic streaming

**Global assignment** determines each point's biome independently from its world coordinates—temperature from latitude and altitude, moisture from noise or simulation, biome from lookup table. This is the dominant approach for infinite or streaming worlds because any chunk can be generated without knowledge of its neighbors. Minecraft's multi-noise system is purely global: six noise functions evaluated at a position yield a biome with no neighbor queries (minecraft.wiki). Patel's terrain-from-noise approach is similarly global. The advantage is determinism and streamability; the disadvantage is that without careful parameter tuning, global assignment can produce implausible adjacencies (e.g., small desert patches in a wet region where noise values happen to cross a threshold).

**Local assignment** determines biomes per-region based on neighbors, constraints, or designer placement. A designer might place biome volumes in a scene, or an algorithm might assign biomes by traversing the adjacency graph with constraint propagation (e.g., "a desert region must be adjacent to at most one water region"). The Unity Vista system supports "Local Procedural Biome" components placed in scenes (Pinwheel Studio documentation). Dwarf Fortress uses a hybrid: global temperature and rainfall fields provide base values, but world generation rejects and regenerates maps whose biome distributions fail to meet parameter constraints (Adams, Game AI Pro 2). The tradeoff is clear: local assignment offers designer control and guaranteed adjacency rules but requires pre-computation or finite map extent. It does not support infinite chunk streaming without a pre-generated global plan.

Most practical systems combine both. Patel's mapgen4 is a hybrid: the wind-moisture simulation is global (each region's humidity depends on upwind regions), but the simulation runs over a finite pre-generated Voronoi mesh with explicit adjacency—it is not chunk-streamable. Minecraft uses global noise for the base biome but applies local rules for specific features (beaches only at coast, rivers in valleys). The Little Martian devlog uses a two-scale hybrid: a slow-varying "base temperature" noise (changing by a maximum of 0.01 per chunk) for global patterns combined with fast-varying local temperature noise for regional variation (little-martian.dev). Both local and global assignment produce the same output—a biome label per region or per point—that feeds into the same downstream blending and placement systems. The choice affects pipeline architecture: global assignment supports lazy chunk generation, while local assignment requires an upfront pass over the full map graph.

## A typical biome generation pipeline from heightmap to final assignment

A complete biome pipeline consumes the terrain heightmap and produces per-region biome labels with transition weights, following these stages:

1. **Partition terrain into regions** — generate seed points (Poisson disc or jittered grid), compute Delaunay triangulation, derive Voronoi polygons, optionally apply Lloyd's relaxation (1–2 iterations), and build the dual-mesh adjacency graph.
2. **Assign elevation per region** — sample the heightmap at polygon centers and corners, or compute elevation as distance from coast; redistribute elevations to match a desired distribution curve.
3. **Classify land and water** — threshold elevation at sea level; flood fill from map borders through water regions to identify ocean; remaining water components are lakes (connected component analysis).
4. **Compute temperature field** — derive from latitude (equator warm, poles cold) and elevation (lapse rate of ~6.5°C/km), with optional noise perturbation.
5. **Compute moisture field** — either sample independent noise, compute distance from freshwater sources, or run wind-based moisture simulation (evaporation → wind transport → orographic precipitation) across the adjacency graph sorted by wind direction.
6. **Assign biomes** — look up each region's biome from a Whittaker-style temperature × moisture table; apply special overrides (beaches at coast, ice at poles) via additional rules.
7. **Blend transitions** — compute per-point biome weights using scattered blend or noise-distorted boundaries; output weight maps for each biome at each location.

Data flows unidirectionally: the heightmap feeds elevation assignment, which feeds land/water classification and temperature computation. Temperature and moisture together feed the Whittaker lookup. The biome labels and weight maps are the primary outputs consumed by the placement pipeline, which uses them to select asset palettes, density parameters, and placement rules per region.

**Five common mistakes** that produce incorrect or unconvincing biomes:

The most frequent error is **using biome noise at the same frequency as terrain noise**, which creates fragmented microbiomes that flicker between types at the scale of individual terrain features. Biome-controlling noise should operate at wavelengths 4–8× longer than terrain noise. A second mistake is **omitting the rainshadow entirely**, assigning moisture from independent noise that has no correlation with terrain elevation—this produces deserts on mountaintops and rainforests in rain shadows, breaking ecological plausibility. Third, **hard-coded elevation thresholds without redistribution** cause biome bands to shift unpredictably when terrain generation parameters change; redistributing elevation values to match a target cumulative distribution (e.g., Patel's `y(x) = 1 - (1-x)²`) stabilizes biome proportions. Fourth, **discontinuous biome transitions** where blending is absent or uses too small a radius produce visible biome borders that look like political boundaries rather than ecological gradients. Fifth, **failing to separate ocean from lakes** via connected component analysis leads to inland water bodies being classified as ocean and receiving ocean biomes, or coastal regions being misclassified as lakeside.

For placement pipelines, the outputs that feed directly into object placement are: per-region biome label, per-point biome weight maps (for transition zones), per-region moisture value (controls vegetation density), per-region elevation (controls tree line, snow line), and the adjacency graph (for coordinating placement across region boundaries).

## Source landscape

**Interactive tools and demos:**
Amit Patel's mapgen4 (redblobgames.com/maps/mapgen4/) is the only publicly available interactive tool that combines user-painted terrain with live wind-moisture simulation and biome assignment—no other tool lets you observe rainshadow formation in real time. Jason Davies' Lloyd's relaxation demo (jasondavies.com/lloyd/) provides the clearest interactive visualization of centroidal Voronoi tessellation convergence. Azgaar's Fantasy Map Generator (azgaar.github.io/Fantasy-Map-Generator/) is the most feature-complete open-source tool for full-pipeline world generation including climate, biomes, rivers, and political regions.

**Tutorials and dev blogs:**
Patel's "Polygonal Map Generation for Games" (2010, updated 2025) remains the definitive tutorial for Voronoi-based map generation, uniquely providing both the dual-graph architecture and complete pipeline from seed points through biome assignment. The Cuberite generator documentation (cuberite.xoft.cz/docs/Generator.html) is the only source that traces the progressive evolution from trivial biome generators through Voronoi to temperature-humidity models, making the design rationale for each upgrade explicit. Scott Turner's "Here Dragons Abound" blog (heredragonsabound.blogspot.com) provides the most detailed wind-vector simulation on irregular Voronoi meshes, covering failure modes no other source addresses. Nick McDonald's procedural weather article (nickmcd.me, 2018) is unique in treating climate as a coupled differential equation system with emergent phase transitions. The noiseposti.ng article on scattered biome blending (2021) is the only source that rigorously compares grid-based, Gaussian, and scattered-point blending with analysis of grid-artifact causes.

**Academic papers:**
Fortune's sweep-line algorithm paper (1986) establishes the O(n log n) lower bound for Voronoi computation that all practical implementations target. Lloyd's 1982 IEEE paper defines the iterative centroid relaxation that underpins every Voronoi-based map generator's preprocessing step. Fischer et al.'s AutoBiomes paper (*The Visual Computer*, 2020) is the only peer-reviewed publication combining procedural terrain with a complete simplified climate simulation (wind, evaporation, orographic precipitation) validated in a real-time game engine. The Bowyer (1981) and Watson (1981) papers, published in the same journal issue, define the incremental Delaunay insertion algorithm that remains the most widely implemented triangulation method in PCG.

**Game-specific technical documentation:**
Tarn Adams' chapter in Game AI Pro 2 (Chapter 41) provides the only first-person account of iterative world-generation design principles from a major PCG title (Dwarf Fortress), with the critical insight that composing independent scalar fields (temperature, rainfall, drainage) and letting their interplay determine biomes produces "much better results" than direct biome spawning. The Minecraft Wiki world generation pages (minecraft.wiki) provide the most detailed documentation of a shipped multi-noise biome system, including exact parameter ranges, the 6D nearest-neighbor biome selection algorithm, and the complete biome lookup tables for all terrain categories. Alan Zucconi's analysis of Minecraft world generation (2022) is the best secondary source explaining the layer-based biome system's evolution and why the multi-noise replacement was necessary.
