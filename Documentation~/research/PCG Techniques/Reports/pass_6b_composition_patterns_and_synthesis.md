# Pass 6b: Composition Patterns, Synthesis, and Final Reference

This document synthesizes the findings from passes 1a through 6a into a unified reference for composing procedural content generation techniques into working pipelines. It covers how techniques connect, what data flows between them, where boundary failures occur, and how to sequence learning across the full technique library. No new techniques are introduced; the value is entirely in the connections, tradeoffs, and architectural guidance that individual pass documents cannot provide.

---

## Part 1 — Composition Patterns

### 1. Noise + masks + biome assignment

This combination works because each stage contributes a capability the others lack. Scalar noise fields (fBm, ridged multifractal, domain-warped variants) produce continuous spatial variation with naturalistic spectral character, but they have no semantic meaning — a value of 0.7 is just a number. Smoothstep and power-curve thresholding converts these raw fields into masks with controlled falloff, introducing the concept of "regions" without hard boundaries. Whittaker biome classification then maps two or more independent mask axes (temperature, moisture, elevation) to discrete ecological categories, giving semantic meaning to the continuous fields. Without noise, biomes would be geometric shapes; without masks, transitions would be either binary or uncontrolled; without the classification lookup, the system would produce pretty gradients with no gameplay-relevant meaning.

The stage order is: (1) evaluate independent noise fields at each sample point — typically one field per biome axis (elevation, temperature, moisture), each with its own seed, frequency, and octave count — producing scalar values in roughly [−1, 1] or [0, 1]; (2) normalize and reshape each field via power redistribution, smoothstep, or spline remapping to produce masks in [0, 1] with the desired statistical distribution (e.g., exponent > 1 to create more lowland than highland); (3) feed pairs of mask values into a Whittaker-style lookup table that returns a biome enum plus, at transition boundaries, blend weights for the two or three nearest biomes. The intermediate data structures are: raw scalar field (float per sample) → mask (float[0,1] per sample) → biome ID (enum) + blend weights (float array, one per nearby biome).

Three boundary mistakes recur at the junctions between these stages. First, **correlated seeds between noise fields**: if elevation and moisture noise share a seed or use seeds that are simple increments of each other, the two axes will produce correlated spatial patterns, collapsing the 2D Whittaker space into a near-1D line and eliminating most biome diversity. The fix is to use large prime-separated seeds or entirely independent hash seeds per axis. Second, **mismatched frequency between biome noise and terrain noise**: if the biome classification axes operate at the same spatial frequency as the terrain heightmap, biomes will track individual hills and valleys rather than forming coherent regions. Biome noise frequency should be 4–8× slower than terrain detail frequency — Minecraft's multi-noise system enforces this by using separate, much lower-frequency noise fields for temperature and humidity than for surface shape. Third, **dynamic range collapse from cascaded mask multiplication**: when multiple masks are multiplied together (elevation mask × moisture mask × slope mask), the resulting composite tends toward zero everywhere because each multiplication can only reduce values. The output has narrow effective range and poor biome discrimination. The fix is to use additive composition or independent axis evaluation rather than chained multiplication, or to renormalize after each composition step.


### 2. Heightmap + erosion + rivers

Noise-based heightmaps produce elevation with correct spectral character but lack the geomorphological signatures that make terrain look weathered: V-shaped river valleys, U-shaped thermal scree slopes, alluvial fans, and drainage-carved ridge networks. Erosion simulation adds these signatures by physically moving material across the heightmap surface. River generation then identifies the drainage network that erosion has implied but not explicitly marked, converting accumulated flow patterns into discrete water features with consistent topology. Without erosion, terrain looks "pristine" — geologically implausible. Without explicit river extraction, the erosion channels exist as subtle height variations that the renderer may not detect, and water placement becomes arbitrary.

The pipeline proceeds: (1) construct the base heightmap from fBm with island masking and mountain shaping (Pass 2a), producing a float array in [0, 1]; (2) run hydraulic erosion (70K–200K Lague-method droplets on a 512² map) to carve channels and deposit sediment, modifying the heightmap in-place and optionally producing auxiliary sediment-depth and erosion-amount maps; (3) run thermal erosion (50–200 iterations, talus threshold 4/N) to create scree slopes and soften hydraulic channels; (4) run Priority-Flood depression filling (Barnes 2014, with +ε gradient enforcement) on the eroded heightmap to eliminate local minima that would trap water; (5) compute D8 flow directions (steepest downslope neighbor, diagonal distances divided by √2) producing a per-cell direction enum; (6) accumulate flow from highest to lowest cells, producing a per-cell upstream-area count; (7) threshold flow accumulation to extract river segments as polylines with Strahler ordering.

The critical boundary mistakes are: **erosion timing relative to mountain shaping** — if erosion runs before mountain shaping applies ridged-multifractal peaks and regional masks, the erosion will carve channels into terrain that subsequently gets overwritten by mountain placement, wasting computation and producing inconsistent results. Mountain shaping must precede erosion. **Depression filling destroying erosion features** — Priority-Flood raises every local minimum to the level of its lowest outlet, which can fill the very valleys and basins that erosion carefully carved. The +ε variant mitigates this by imposing a minimal downhill gradient rather than flattening to the outlet level, but aggressive ε values create artificial ramps. The developer must tune ε to be large enough for unambiguous flow direction but small enough to preserve erosion-carved detail, typically 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁴ of the heightmap's total elevation range. **River threshold sensitivity to heightmap resolution** — flow accumulation counts upstream cells, so a river threshold that produces correct streams on a 512² map will produce either no rivers (threshold too high) or river soup (threshold too low) on a 1024² map, because doubling resolution roughly quadruples upstream cell counts for equivalent watersheds. The threshold must scale with resolution, approximately as the square of the resolution ratio.


### 3. Voronoi regions + graph connectivity

Voronoi partitioning and Delaunay triangulation produce spatial regions with well-defined boundaries and a natural adjacency structure, but they encode no semantic information — every polygon is interchangeable. Overworld graph construction adds meaning by labeling nodes with biome types, progression roles, and difficulty tiers, and by pruning the dense Delaunay adjacency into a navigable connectivity structure with controlled loop density. Without Voronoi, regions are either axis-aligned rectangles (BSP) or noise-driven blobs without clean boundaries. Without the graph layer, region connectivity is determined entirely by spatial proximity, preventing any designed progression.

The pipeline is: (1) generate seed points via Poisson disk sampling with minimum distance r controlling average region size; (2) compute Delaunay triangulation (via Delaunator or equivalent), producing a triangle mesh whose edges define maximal adjacency; (3) derive dual Voronoi polygons (using circumcenters or centroids — centroids are preferred because circumcenters can fall outside obtuse triangles, producing degenerate polygons); (4) construct the region adjacency graph directly from the Delaunay edges, where each edge connects two Voronoi polygon IDs; (5) extract the minimum spanning tree from this adjacency graph to guarantee connectivity; (6) add back 10–15% of non-MST Delaunay edges to create loops and alternative paths, selecting edges that create the shortest cycles (for interesting topology) or that connect distant subgraphs (for shortcuts); (7) assign biome labels, progression tiers, and feature flags to each graph node based on designer rules or procedural criteria.

Boundary mistakes at the Voronoi-to-graph handoff include: **cell irregularity distorting graph edge weights** — if Voronoi cells vary greatly in area (which happens when Poisson disk seeds have high variance or when boundary effects compress edge cells), graph edges between large and small cells represent very different physical distances. Algorithms that treat all edges as equivalent (unweighted MST, uniform-cost pathfinding) will produce topologically correct but spatially distorted graphs where "nearby" nodes in the graph may be far apart on the map. Edge weights should be proportional to the Euclidean distance between cell centroids. **Non-planar graph from reconnection** — adding back edges after MST extraction can create edge crossings that make the graph non-planar. If downstream systems (Edgar's dungeon layout, force-directed graph drawing) require planarity, the reconnection step must check for crossings before accepting each edge, using a segment intersection test against all existing edges. **Graph connectivity vs. spatial proximity mismatch** — two regions that are spatially adjacent (sharing a Voronoi edge) might not be connected in the pruned overworld graph, creating situations where the player can see an adjacent region but cannot reach it without a long detour. This is a feature for gated progression but a bug for open-world exploration. The designer must decide which Voronoi adjacencies are mandatory connections versus optional shortcuts, and the MST must be seeded to preserve critical spatial adjacencies.


### 4. Cellular automata + cleanup pass

Cellular automata cave generation produces organic, natural-looking cave shapes through iterated local rules, but it provides no connectivity guarantee — the random initial fill and local smoothing rules routinely produce multiple disconnected caverns. The cleanup pass (flood fill + corridor drilling or component culling) repairs connectivity while attempting to preserve the organic character that makes CA caves visually appealing. Without CA, caves are either rectangular (BSP) or noise-blob-shaped; without the cleanup pass, generated caves will contain unreachable areas that waste content and may strand the player.

The pipeline is: (1) initialize a tile grid with random wall/floor at 40–45% initial fill density; (2) iterate the CA rule (typically B5678/S45678, meaning a cell becomes wall if 5+ of its 8 neighbors are walls, floor otherwise) for 15 iterations, or use the enhanced two-phase variant (R1≥5 OR R2≤2 for 4 iterations, then R1≥5 for 3 iterations) for larger, more connected caverns; (3) run flood fill from a designated spawn point (or from the largest connected component) to label all connected components and compute their sizes; (4) either cull components below a minimum size threshold (replacing them with wall) or drill corridors from each disconnected component to the main component (typically via A* or straight-line carving toward the nearest floor cell of the target component). The intermediate data structures are: binary tile grid (int[][]) → connected component label grid (int[][]) + component size list → repaired tile grid.

Boundary failures include: **corridor drilling destroying CA's organic character** — straight-line or L-shaped corridors between components create obviously artificial passages through otherwise natural-looking cave walls. Mitigation strategies include using A* with a randomized cost function (adding noise to the pathfinding cost so corridors meander), running a few additional CA iterations after corridor drilling to smooth the corridor edges, or using agent-based tunneling with noise-driven direction changes. **Flood fill seed placement determining which component survives** — if the spawn point is placed randomly and happens to land in a small component, the cleanup pass will treat that small component as "main" and either cull or drill to all larger components, producing an oddly shaped result dominated by narrow corridors. The spawn should be placed in the largest component (identified by a preliminary flood fill pass), or the largest component should be designated as main regardless of spawn location. **Resolution scaling interaction with connectivity** — Jeremy Kun's 4× upscale technique (replacing each cell with a 4×4 block, then running additional CA iterations at the higher resolution for smoother walls) can disconnect components that were connected at the lower resolution, because the additional CA iterations at higher resolution may close narrow single-cell passages. A second connectivity verification must run after upscaling.


### 5. WFC + designer-authored constraints

WFC's strength is producing locally consistent tilemap output where every neighborhood matches the adjacency rules, but unconstrained WFC has no sense of purpose — it generates valid but aimless patterns. Designer-authored constraints inject intent: fixed tiles for entrances and exits, boundary conditions at grid edges, path connectivity requirements, and critical-path room types from a mission graph. Without WFC, maintaining global adjacency consistency requires either hand-placement or greedy algorithms that frequently fail. Without designer constraints, WFC output lacks the goal-directed structure that makes a space playable.

The pipeline is: (1) define the tile set with per-tile weights and adjacency rules (socket labels or sample-extracted pairs); (2) set up the WFC domain arrays — `wave[x][y][t]` booleans and `support[x][y][d][t]` counts — initialized to all-possible; (3) apply designer constraints by pre-collapsing fixed cells (entrance tile at position A, exit tile at position B) and banning tiles at boundary cells (e.g., no open-edge tiles at the grid perimeter); (4) if using DeBroglie-style path constraints, add non-local constraints requiring connected paths between specified endpoints; (5) if a mission graph from Pass 6a provides room type requirements, seed specific grid regions with tile subsets corresponding to each room type; (6) run the WFC observe-propagate loop: select the minimum-entropy uncollapsed cell, collapse it to a weighted-random tile, propagate support-count decrements via AC-4 until stable; (7) on contradiction, either restart (Gumin's approach) or backtrack using a changelog (DeBroglie's approach).

Boundary mistakes at the constraint-WFC junction: **fixed tiles creating destructive propagation cascades** — pre-collapsing a cell to a specific tile triggers a propagation wave that can eliminate large swaths of the possibility space, potentially over-constraining distant cells and causing contradictions. This is especially severe when fixed tiles have restrictive adjacency rules (e.g., a door tile that can only neighbor wall tiles on three sides). The mitigation is to fix tiles in order from least-constrained to most-constrained, and to test each fixation's propagation impact before committing. **Path constraints requiring expensive backtracking** — DeBroglie's Connected constraint must verify that a connected path exists between endpoints after every collapse, which in the worst case requires a graph search per collapse step. For large grids (64×64+), this dominates runtime. The practical fix is to check path connectivity only periodically (every N collapses) or to use a union-find data structure for incremental connectivity tracking. **Mission graph room types lacking matching tile subsets** — if the mission graph specifies "boss arena" but the tileset has no tiles specifically designed for large open rooms, the WFC will either fail to realize the room type or produce a generic fill indistinguishable from corridors. The tileset and the mission graph's room vocabulary must be co-designed; this is a content pipeline constraint, not an algorithmic one.


### 6. World graph + local terrain realization

The overworld graph defines what the world contains at the macro scale — which regions exist, their biome types, progression roles, and how they connect — but it has no geometry. Local terrain generators (fBm heightmaps, CA caves, BSP dungeons) produce geometry but have no awareness of the world's narrative structure. Composing them assigns each graph node's parameters to a local generator, producing a world where macro structure is designed and micro detail is procedural. Without the graph, the world is a seamless but purposeless expanse. Without local generators, the world is a meaningful but abstract diagram.

The pipeline is: (1) construct the overworld graph with biome labels, elevation ranges, feature flags (dungeon, settlement, wilderness), and progression tiers per node; (2) partition the world space into regions corresponding to graph nodes (via Voronoi from node positions, or rectangular subdivision); (3) for each region, select the appropriate local generator based on node type — fBm heightmap with region-specific frequency, persistence, and power-curve parameters for terrain nodes; BSP or room-and-corridor layout for dungeon nodes; settlement placement pipeline for town nodes; (4) generate each region's content using the node's parameters as constraints; (5) stitch region boundaries by blending heightmaps across shared Voronoi edges, aligning dungeon entrances with terrain surfaces, and ensuring roads from Pass 5b cross region boundaries at compatible elevations.

The boundary concerns are the most architecturally challenging of any combination. **Terrain parameter discontinuity at region boundaries** — if adjacent nodes specify different fBm parameters (octave count, persistence, base frequency), the generated heightmaps will differ in character at the shared boundary even after biome blending. Blending height values across the boundary produces smooth elevation but cannot blend the *texture* of the terrain (one side has sharp ridges, the other has rolling hills). The fix is to interpolate not just height values but generator parameters across a transition zone, or to use a single base heightmap for the entire world with region-specific post-processing. **Dungeon entrances misaligned with terrain surfaces** — if a dungeon node generates its layout independently of the surrounding terrain, the entrance room's floor elevation may not match the terrain surface at the dungeon's map position. The dungeon generator must receive the terrain height at the entrance point as an input constraint, and the terrain generator must carve a compatible opening (cave mouth, staircase, clearing) at that location. **Graph edge traversability not matching realized terrain** — the overworld graph says region A connects to region B, implying a traversable path exists between them, but the local terrain generators may produce an impassable mountain range or river at the shared boundary. Either the terrain generator must guarantee a traversable corridor along each graph edge (by masking passability along the edge polyline), or the graph must be validated against the realized terrain and edges removed or rerouted where terrain blocks passage.


### 7. Poisson disk sampling + slope and biome filtering

Poisson disk sampling produces spatially uniform point distributions with controlled minimum spacing, but real-world object placement varies with terrain and ecology — dense forest in valleys, sparse scrub on ridgelines, no vegetation on cliffs or in water. The slope and biome filtering pipeline transforms the uniform Poisson distribution into an ecologically plausible placement by modulating density and rejecting candidates based on terrain properties. Without Poisson disk, placement suffers from clumping and gaps. Without filtering, trees appear on cliff faces and cacti grow underwater.

The pipeline is: (1) evaluate the density field at each point on the map, compositing biome density weights, noise variation, slope masks, and exclusion masks into a single [0, 1] value (Pass 5b); (2) generate candidate points via Bridson's algorithm with either radius modulation (min_dist varies inversely with density) or fixed radius with rejection probability (reject with probability 1 − density); (3) for each surviving candidate, compute terrain slope via central finite differences on the heightmap and reject or attenuate candidates exceeding the per-object-type slope threshold, using a smoothstep ramp rather than a hard cutoff; (4) apply altitude band filtering with similarly feathered thresholds; (5) apply exclusion masks (water, roads, buildings, previously placed large objects) by checking each candidate against the composite exclusion field and rejecting where the mask value falls below a threshold.

The boundary failures specific to this composition: **density-boundary clumping** — when Poisson disk radius modulation produces different minimum spacings on either side of a biome edge, points near the boundary from the dense side can be closer to points on the sparse side than the sparse side's minimum distance would allow. The result is a visible line of clumped objects at biome transitions. The fix is to use the maximum of the two adjacent biomes' minimum distances for points near the boundary, or to run a post-placement minimum-distance cull. **Slope mask resolution mismatch with placement grid** — if the slope is computed from a heightmap at lower resolution than the Poisson disk background grid, narrow ridgelines or cliff bands fall between slope samples and objects are incorrectly placed on steep terrain. The slope computation resolution must equal or exceed the placement grid resolution. **Exclusion mask not updated between hierarchical passes** — when large objects (buildings, large trees) are placed first and small objects (shrubs, rocks) are placed second, the exclusion mask must incorporate the footprints of all objects placed in earlier passes. If the exclusion mask is computed once before the first pass and not updated, small objects will overlap large objects' footprints. The mask must be incrementally updated (via footprint rasterization or spatial hash insertion) after each hierarchical placement pass.


### 8. Random walk + room graph scaffolding

Random walk generators (drunkard's walk, agent diggers) produce organic, cave-like spaces with guaranteed single-origin connectivity, but they provide no control over room topology, progression structure, or the distinction between rooms and corridors. Graph-driven scaffolding (from Pass 3b) provides exactly this topological control — hub-and-spoke layouts, cycles for lock-and-key gating, dead-end branches for optional treasure — but produces abstract nodes and edges with no organic character. Composing them yields dungeons that are both organically shaped and structurally sound. The composition can flow in either direction: walk-then-extract-graph, or graph-then-walk-to-realize.

In the **walk-then-extract** direction: (1) run one or more random walk agents on a solid grid with tuned parameters (lifetime, turn probability, room-carving probability from Pass 3a); (2) identify room regions in the carved output via flood fill on open areas wider than a corridor threshold (e.g., 4+ contiguous floor cells in both dimensions); (3) build a connectivity graph where room regions are nodes and corridors connecting them are edges; (4) annotate the graph with lock-and-key structures from Pass 3b. In the **graph-then-walk** direction: (1) generate the mission/progression graph (Dormans-style grammar or designer-authored); (2) place graph nodes as target positions on the tile grid; (3) run walk agents constrained to connect graph nodes — agents spawn at one node and walk toward the next, carving as they go, with noise-driven deviation from the direct path; (4) run additional unconstrained walks from hub nodes to create organic side passages.

Boundary failures: **extracted graph topology too simple for lock-and-key systems** — random walk output tends toward tree-like topology (a single branching path from the origin) because the walk rarely crosses its own path to create loops. Lock-and-key progression requires cycles (the player must find a key on one branch before returning to unlock a door on another). If the extracted graph is a pure tree, every key is trivially encountered before its lock, eliminating puzzle structure. The fix is to run multiple walk agents from different starting points (creating merges and loops) or to post-process the graph by adding shortcut edges between spatially adjacent but graph-distant rooms. **Room boundaries ambiguous in organic walk output** — a random walk does not distinguish "room" from "wide corridor." Any region-detection algorithm (flood fill with area thresholds, morphological opening) will produce noisy, inconsistent room boundaries that shift with parameter tuning. This ambiguity propagates into the graph: is that wide passage a room (node) or a corridor (edge)? The developer must establish clear geometric criteria for room detection before extracting the graph, and accept that some organic features will be misclassified. **Graph-imposed connectivity conflicting with walk's organic character** — when agents are constrained to connect specific graph nodes, the walk becomes a glorified pathfinding algorithm between waypoints, losing the exploratory organic character that motivates using walks in the first place. The balance is to give agents loose constraints (walk generally toward the target with high turn probability and no collision avoidance) rather than tight ones (A* path with noise).

## Part 2 — Data Structure Catalog

### Scalar field

A scalar field maps spatial coordinates to a single floating-point value representing any continuous quantity: elevation, temperature, moisture, density, or probability. In a game context, scalar fields are stored as 2D float arrays at chunk resolution (typically 64×64 to 256×256 per chunk), as 3D voxel grids for volumetric terrain, or evaluated procedurally per-sample in shaders without intermediate storage. Every noise primitive from Pass 1a (Perlin, OpenSimplex2, Worley, value noise) produces scalar fields, and every compositional technique from Pass 1b (fBm, ridged multifractal, turbulence, domain warping) both consumes and produces them. Downstream, scalar fields drive heightmap construction (Pass 2a), biome classification (Pass 5a, as axes in Whittaker lookup), density fields for object placement (Pass 5b), and cave density functions (Pass 2b). The primary conversion concern is normalization: noise functions output values in different ranges (Perlin: approximately [−1, 1]; fBm: range depends on octave count and gain; ridged multifractal: [0, 1] after offset), and downstream consumers expect [0, 1]. Cascading unnormalized fields through multiplication or addition produces unpredictable ranges and is the most common source of "flat terrain" or "all-water" bugs.

### Mask

A mask is a scalar field constrained to [0, 1] (soft mask) or {0, 1} (binary mask) that controls where an effect applies. Masks are produced by thresholding scalar fields (smoothstep on elevation, slope, or noise value), by distance functions (island masks from Pass 2a, SDF-derived boundaries from Pass 1b), and by rasterizing geometric features (road corridors, building footprints, water bodies from Pass 5b). Masks are consumed by scalar field composition (Pass 1b, as blend weights between terrain types), object placement filtering (Pass 5b, exclusion masks), erosion region control (Pass 2a, restricting erosion to specific elevation bands), and biome transition blending (Pass 5a). The critical compatibility issue is composition order: masks are typically combined by multiplication (AND logic), which drives values toward zero monotonically. Three multiplicative masks each averaging 0.7 produce a composite averaging 0.34 — much sparser than any individual mask intended. When masks are layered from multiple passes, the developer must track the cumulative effect on dynamic range and consider additive or max-based composition for masks that should be inclusive rather than restrictive.

### Tile grid

A tile grid is a 2D integer array where each cell holds an enum value representing a discrete state: wall, floor, door, water, or a specific tile type index. Tile grids are produced by BSP dungeon generation (Pass 3a), cellular automata (Pass 3a, Pass 2b), random walk carving (Pass 3a), room-and-corridor generators (Pass 3a), WFC (Pass 4b), and room template placement (Pass 3a). They are consumed by autotiling (Pass 4a, converting logical states to visual tile indices), connectivity verification (Pass 3a, flood fill on floor cells), graph extraction (Pass 3b, identifying rooms and corridors as nodes and edges), and rendering. The major conversion issue is that different generators use different cell semantics: CA caves use binary (wall/floor), BSP generators distinguish wall/floor/door/corridor, WFC uses tile-type indices that map to visual assets. Any pipeline combining outputs from multiple generators must establish a unified cell vocabulary and convert at each handoff. A subtler issue is cell resolution: CA caves at 80×80 produce very different gameplay than BSP rooms at 80×80, because the "grain" of organic shapes versus rectangular rooms implies different minimum feature sizes.

### Voxel grid

A voxel grid is a chunked 3D float array (typically 16³ or 32³ per chunk) where each cell holds a density or signed distance value. Positive values represent solid material, negative values represent empty space (or vice versa, depending on convention), and the zero-isosurface defines the terrain boundary. Voxel grids are produced by converting 2D heightmaps to 3D density functions (density = surfaceHeight(x,z) − y + noise3D, from Pass 2b), by 3D noise fields for cave generation (dual-noise intersection from Pass 2b), and by SDF Boolean operations (Pass 1b). They are consumed by mesh extraction algorithms: marching cubes, dual contouring, or surface nets (all Pass 2b), and by cave carving operations (multiplying terrain density by cave mask density). The primary compatibility concern is sign convention: marching cubes implementations disagree on whether positive or negative is "inside," and mixing a voxel grid from one convention with an extractor from the other produces inverted terrain (solid sky, hollow ground). A second concern is the 1-voxel overlap at chunk boundaries required for correct mesh extraction — each chunk must store one extra layer of voxels from each neighbor to compute correct isosurface vertices at edges.

### Graph (general)

A graph is a set of nodes connected by edges, stored as an adjacency list (array of arrays), adjacency matrix (for dense graphs), or edge list. Graphs are produced by Delaunay triangulation (Pass 5a, from seed points), MST extraction (Pass 3a, Pass 5b, from Delaunay), mission grammar rewriting (Pass 3b, from seed chain), overworld graph construction (Pass 6a, from region nodes), and connectivity extraction from spatial generators (Pass 3b, from carved tile grids). Graphs are consumed by room connectivity planning (Pass 3a, for corridor routing), road network layout (Pass 5b, MST + A* per edge), progression gating analysis (Pass 3b, lock-and-key traversal simulation), critical path extraction (Pass 6a), and region adjacency-based biome assignment (Pass 5a). The central conversion issue is directed versus undirected: progression graphs (mission graphs, lock-and-key structures) require directed edges to encode ordering, while spatial adjacency graphs (Delaunay, Voronoi dual) are inherently undirected. Converting an undirected spatial graph to a directed progression graph requires adding direction based on distance from the start node, elevation gradient, or designer annotation — this directionality assignment is where spatial and structural graph semantics diverge.

### Point set

A point set is an unordered collection of 2D or 3D spatial coordinates, stored as a flat array of floats or as entries in a spatial hash grid for fast neighbor queries. Point sets are produced by Poisson disk sampling (Pass 1a, Bridson's algorithm), stratified jittered sampling (Pass 1a), uniform random sampling, and settlement placement scoring (Pass 5b). They are consumed by Voronoi/Delaunay construction (Pass 5a, as seed points), object placement pipelines (Pass 5b, as candidate positions for trees, rocks, buildings), and density field evaluation (sampling the density at each candidate point). The key conversion note is that Poisson disk points carry only position data; downstream passes must attach additional attributes (object type, scale, rotation, biome membership) by evaluating terrain properties at each point's location. When a point set is generated for Voronoi seeds, the minimum distance r determines average cell size, and changing r changes the entire partitioning — there is no way to add or remove individual points without regenerating the Voronoi.

### Spline

A spline is an ordered sequence of control points with interpolation metadata (spline type, tension, parameterization), representing a smooth curve through space. Splines are typically stored as arrays of 2D or 3D positions plus per-segment type tags. They are produced by road routing (Pass 5b, Catmull-Rom fitting after A* pathfinding with Ramer-Douglas-Peucker simplification), river path extraction (Pass 2b, connecting flow-accumulation segments), and designer-authored paths. They are consumed by road mesh generation (Pass 5b, extruding road geometry along the spline), terrain modification (Pass 5b, excavation and embankment within the road corridor), and exclusion mask generation (Pass 5b, rasterizing the spline with buffer width to prevent object placement on roads). The compatibility concern is parameterization: Catmull-Rom splines require centripetal parameterization (α = 0.5) to prevent self-intersection at sharp turns, but many implementations default to uniform parameterization (α = 0), which produces loops at acute control point angles. Additionally, splines from river extraction (which follow terrain drainage) and splines from road routing (which follow cost-optimized paths) have different smoothness characteristics and should not share fitting parameters.

### Signed distance field

A signed distance field stores the signed Euclidean distance to the nearest surface at each sample point — negative inside, zero at the boundary, positive outside. SDFs are stored as 2D or 3D float arrays or evaluated procedurally from analytic primitives. They are produced by SDF primitive composition (Pass 1b, circles, boxes, segments combined via smooth Boolean operations) and by rasterizing existing geometry into a distance grid. They are consumed by mesh extraction (Pass 2b, marching cubes on the zero-isosurface), collision detection (distance value = penetration depth), proximity-based effects (shore foam, cliff moss), and mask generation (thresholding at various distances produces concentric mask bands). The major conversion issue is that Boolean operations (union via min, subtraction via max(−a, b)) corrupt interior distances — the distance magnitude inside the shape is no longer exact after these operations. For mesh extraction via marching cubes this is acceptable because only the zero-crossing matters, but for any downstream use that relies on interior distance magnitude (e.g., wall thickness calculation, interior ambient occlusion), the corrupted values produce errors.

### Region map

A region map assigns each cell in a 2D grid to a region identifier (integer ID), defining a spatial partition where every cell belongs to exactly one region. Region maps are produced by Voronoi partitioning (Pass 5a, cell ID from nearest seed point), BSP subdivision (Pass 5a, leaf node ID), flood fill connected component analysis (Pass 5a, labeling contiguous areas of the same terrain type), and watershed segmentation. They are consumed by biome assignment (Pass 5a, applying biome properties per region), adjacency graph construction (scanning region map borders to identify neighboring region pairs), political territory systems (Pass 5b, settlement expansion), and per-region terrain parameter selection (Pass 6a, looking up generator parameters from the region's graph node). The conversion concern is boundary representation: a region map stores only the region ID per cell, not the boundary geometry. Extracting region boundaries (for rendering borders, blending transitions, or placing walls) requires scanning for cells where adjacent cells have different IDs — an O(N) pass that must be repeated if the region map changes.

### Adjacency map

An adjacency map is a graph derived from a region map, where nodes are region IDs and edges connect regions that share at least one cell boundary. It is augmented with border metadata: shared border length, average border elevation, border midpoint position. Adjacency maps are produced automatically from Voronoi-Delaunay duality (Pass 5a, where Delaunay edges correspond exactly to Voronoi polygon adjacencies) or by scanning a region map for boundary-adjacent cell pairs. They are consumed by biome constraint systems (Pass 5a, enforcing that arctic and tropical biomes cannot be adjacent), moisture simulation (Pass 5a, propagating moisture along adjacency edges weighted by shared border length), road network routing (Pass 5b, finding paths through region adjacency), and overworld graph construction (Pass 6a, pruning adjacency to MST + loops). The conversion note is that a Voronoi-derived adjacency map is exact and complete by construction, but a region-map-scanned adjacency map may miss adjacencies where regions touch only at a single corner cell (diagonal adjacency), depending on whether the scan checks 4-connected or 8-connected neighbors.

### Chunk coordinate system

A chunk coordinate system maps spatial positions to discrete chunk identifiers (typically integer pairs (chunkX, chunkZ) for 2D or triples for 3D), enabling a hash map from chunk coordinates to chunk data. It is produced by the chunked generation architecture (Pass 6a) and consumed by lazy generation schedulers (loading/unloading chunks based on player proximity), LOD systems (selecting mesh detail level per chunk based on camera distance), boundary matching systems (identifying neighboring chunks for normal computation and seam stitching), and cross-chunk structure systems (Minecraft's structure_starts/structure_references protocol). The critical compatibility concern is coordinate space: world-space positions must be consistently divided by chunk size to produce chunk coordinates, and all noise evaluations within a chunk must use world-space coordinates (not chunk-local indices) to avoid height seams. A second concern is chunk size selection: Lague's 241-vertex chunks (240 intervals, divisible by 2/4/6/8/10/12) enable clean LOD decimation at six levels, while Minecraft's 16-block chunks optimize for the structure reference system and mob spawning calculations. Chunk size is an architectural decision that propagates to every downstream system and is expensive to change after initial implementation.

### Biome map

A biome map assigns each cell a biome type (enum: desert, grassland, taiga, ocean, etc.) and, at transition zones, blend weights for adjacent biomes. It is stored as a per-cell enum with an optional per-cell float array of blend weights. Biome maps are produced by Whittaker classification (Pass 5a, from temperature and moisture axes), multi-noise selection (Minecraft's 6D parameter space), and designer-painted override maps. They are consumed by terrain material selection (choosing ground textures and vegetation palettes per biome), density field construction (Pass 5b, biome-specific object density weights), heightmap parameter selection (Pass 2a, per-biome frequency and amplitude settings), and dungeon theming (Pass 3b, selecting tile sets and enemy palettes). The key conversion issue is blend representation: a hard biome map (one enum per cell) produces sharp visible transitions, while a blended biome map (weighted mixture) requires all downstream consumers to handle weighted combinations — material shaders must blend textures, placement systems must sample from mixed density tables, and heightmap generators must interpolate parameters. Systems designed for hard biome boundaries cannot consume blended biome maps without modification.

## Part 3 — Techniques by Game Problem

### Continents and coastlines

The standard pipeline is fBm (4–6 octaves, persistence 0.5) → domain warping for organic shapes → island mask (distance function with noise perturbation, amplitude 0.1–0.3) → sea level threshold. Red Blob Games' mapgen2 uses a different approach: Voronoi dual mesh with elevation assigned by distance from coast, computed via BFS. The key tradeoff is noise-first versus mesh-first: noise-first (fBm + mask) is simpler, chunk-streamable, and produces infinite terrain, but coastline shape is statistically random with no designer control. Mesh-first (Voronoi + elevation assignment) gives explicit coastline control and natural drainage structure but requires the entire map to exist before any local detail can be generated.

### Mountains

Ridged multifractal (Musgrave parameters: H=1.0, offset=1.0, gain=2.0) with a regional mask (cubed low-frequency noise at freq 0.002) to localize mountain ranges. Voronoi F2−F1 blended with ridged noise produces ridge-valley structure. Power redistribution (exponent 2.5–3.0) pushes elevation distribution toward the extremes. The tradeoff is between ridged multifractal (heterogeneous, dramatic peaks, but harder to control placement) and fBm with high power redistribution (more uniform, easier to mask into specific regions, but less geologically convincing). Choose ridged when mountains are a primary visual feature; choose shaped fBm when mountains are background and placement control matters more.

### Rivers

Priority-Flood depression filling → D8 flow direction → flow accumulation → threshold for stream segments. Alternative: mapgen4's upstream growth from coast on a Delaunay mesh with wind-based rainfall simulation and Strahler ordering. Andrino's rivers-first approach generates rivers before terrain, then shapes terrain to match. The tradeoff is between drainage-basin rivers (physically plausible, emerge from terrain, but require full heightmap computation before any river can be identified) and pre-placed rivers (designer-controlled placement, terrain adapts to river positions, but requires solving the terrain-river compatibility problem, which has no standardized solution).

### Caves

Three methods with clearly different use cases: cellular automata (2D roguelike, organic shapes, no connectivity guarantee — requires flood fill cleanup), dual-noise intersection (3D voxel, chunk-independent, consistent density, good for streaming worlds), Perlin worms / agent tunneling (3D corridors, designer-controllable width and direction, sequential state prevents chunk independence). The tradeoff is connectivity guarantee versus chunk independence: CA and Perlin worms can guarantee connectivity within a single generation pass but depend on global state, while noise thresholding is perfectly chunk-independent but produces disconnected pockets that require post-verification or tolerance of isolated caverns.

### Biomes

Temperature-moisture Whittaker lookup (Patel: 4 elevation × 6 moisture → 13 biomes) is the default. Minecraft 1.18+ uses 6D multi-noise parameter space for more nuanced classification. Transition blending via KdotJPG's scattered blend (normalized sparse convolution) produces the highest quality boundaries without grid artifacts. The tradeoff is global versus local assignment: global coordinate-based assignment (Minecraft) supports infinite streaming but prevents designer control over specific region identities; local pre-computed assignment (Dwarf Fortress style) enables neighbor-aware constraints (no desert adjacent to tundra) but requires the region structure to exist before any chunk generates.

### Dungeons

BSP or room-and-corridor for explorable spaces with rectangular rooms (simple, reliable, well-documented). Dormans-style mission grammar + spatial realization for progression-gated experiences (lock-and-key, critical path, optional branches). The tradeoff is space-first versus structure-first: space-first generators are implementable in a weekend and produce playable results immediately, but their connectivity is accidental and cannot guarantee designed progression. Structure-first generators produce intentional experiences but require co-design of the grammar, the spatial realization layer, and the tile set — a much larger engineering investment with significantly poorer documentation.

### Villages and settlements

Multi-criteria terrain scoring (flatness, water proximity, resource access, inter-settlement separation) → candidate filtering → Voronoi expansion for territory → political aggregation. Cepero's pipeline is the most complete documented approach. The tradeoff is between simple placement (score terrain, place at maxima, done) and simulation-based growth (settlements expand, compete for territory, build infrastructure). Simple placement is reliable but static; growth simulation produces emergent political geography but has no standardized algorithm — all published approaches are conceptual rather than implementation-ready.

### Roads

Delaunay triangulation of settlement positions → MST for minimum connectivity → add back 10–15% edges for loops → A* on slope/biome cost map per edge → Catmull-Rom spline fitting → terrain modification (excavation/embankment). The tradeoff is between topological simplicity (MST-only, tree structure, one path between any two settlements) and redundancy (additional loop edges, multiple paths, more natural network). MST-only is simple but produces dead-end towns; adding loops is more realistic but requires careful cost-function tuning to prevent roads that redundantly parallel each other. No published source provides tested cost-function weight sets.

### Vegetation

Hierarchical Poisson disk (large trees first at wide spacing → small trees → shrubs → ground cover) with density field modulation, slope filtering via smoothstep ramps, and exclusion masks for water/roads/buildings. The tradeoff is between radius modulation (smooth density gradients, guaranteed minimum spacing, but complex Poisson disk implementation) and rejection probability (simpler implementation, works with any Poisson disk sampler, but no minimum-spacing guarantee near density boundaries, producing the density-boundary clumping artifact).

### World maps

Voronoi dual mesh (Poisson disk seeds → Delaunay → polygons) with elevation assignment, climate simulation, biome classification, and river generation on the mesh structure. Red Blob Games' mapgen2/mapgen4 are the canonical references. Alternative: pure noise-based approach with fBm elevation, independent temperature/moisture noise, and threshold-based biome assignment. The tradeoff is mesh-based versus noise-based: mesh-based produces clean region boundaries, natural drainage, and designer-friendly graph structure, but the entire map must exist at once. Noise-based supports infinite streaming but produces no inherent region structure and requires separate systems for rivers, boundaries, and adjacency.

### Local area maps

Chunk-based generation with coordinate-seeded noise (fBm, domain warping at absolute world coordinates), LOD mesh decimation (vertex skipping at LOD level n, CDLOD morphing for seamless transitions), and boundary matching (extended grid for normals, global normalization for heights). The tradeoff is visual quality versus streaming performance: higher-resolution chunks with more fBm octaves produce better terrain but cost more per chunk and require more aggressive LOD to maintain frame rate. Lague's 241-vertex chunks with 6 LOD levels represent a well-tested balance point.

### Progression spaces

Mission grammar (Dormans-style graph rewriting from seed chain, or Unexplored-style cyclic backbone with ~5,000 rules at three resolution stages) → lock-and-key annotation (Metazelda key-level assignment) → spatial realization (Edgar configuration-space SA, or Unexplored 5×5 grid embedding) → connectivity verification (flood fill + inventory-tracking traversal simulation). The tradeoff is between grammar complexity and output quality: simple grammars (6 lock-key rewrite rules) produce basic gating structures quickly; complex grammars (Unexplored's scale) produce rich, Zelda-quality progression but require months of rule authoring with no published methodology for systematic grammar design. This is the least documented major PCG technique.

## Part 4 — Learning Roadmap

### Phase 1: Noise foundations

**Techniques:** coordinate hashing (SquirrelNoise5), value noise, Perlin noise (improved), OpenSimplex2, fBm (standard, with gain/lacunarity/octave parameters).

This phase must come first because every subsequent technique either evaluates noise directly or consumes a scalar field that noise produces. A developer who understands coordinate hashing can seed any deterministic system; a developer who understands fBm can produce the raw material for terrain, biomes, density fields, and cave volumes. Learning noise first also builds the critical mental model that procedural generation is function evaluation at coordinates, not sequential state mutation — this perspective is prerequisite for understanding chunk-based streaming, coordinate-seeded determinism, and all stateless generation techniques.

**Commonly learned too early:** Worley noise. While it belongs to the noise primitive family, its use cases (cellular patterns, territory maps, ridge extraction via F2−F1) are specialized and do not appear until region partitioning and advanced terrain shaping. Learning it alongside Perlin/simplex creates confusion about when to use which. **Commonly skipped incorrectly:** coordinate hashing. Developers jump to noise functions without understanding stateless random access, then struggle with chunk seeding, deterministic replay, and parallel generation.

### Phase 2: Spatial composition and terrain

**Techniques:** heightmap construction from fBm, power redistribution, island/continent masks, domain warping, Worley noise, Poisson disk sampling (Bridson's algorithm), ridged multifractal.

This phase combines noise primitives into spatially meaningful outputs: shaped terrain, bounded landmasses, organic displacement, and blue-noise point distributions. Domain warping is placed here because it requires fBm comprehension; Poisson disk is placed here because it is the foundation for both object placement and Voronoi seeding in later phases. Ridged multifractal introduces the concept of inter-octave feedback (signal-dependent amplitude), which is a prerequisite for understanding other feedback-driven systems like erosion.

**Commonly learned too early:** signed distance fields. SDFs are mathematically elegant and well-documented, tempting developers to invest heavily before they have terrain or placement systems that would consume SDF output. SDFs are useful but not foundational — they serve as masks and shape primitives within pipelines built from the techniques above.

### Phase 3: Simulation and spatial generation

**Techniques:** hydraulic erosion (Lague particle method), thermal erosion, cellular automata caves, BSP dungeons, room-and-corridor generation, connectivity verification (flood fill, Union-Find), autotiling (bitmask).

This phase introduces techniques that consume the spatial fields from Phase 2 and produce playable geometry. Erosion transforms heightmaps into geomorphologically convincing terrain. CA caves and BSP dungeons produce the first complete, playable game spaces. Connectivity verification teaches the critical lesson that generation must be validated — not all output is usable. Autotiling bridges the gap between logical tile grids and visual rendering.

**Commonly learned too early:** WFC. Developers encounter WFC through viral Twitter/X posts and attempt to use it before understanding the adjacency-rule data structure it operates on, the constraint propagation algorithm it depends on, or the tile grid semantics it produces. WFC should be learned only after autotiling and adjacency rules are understood, because WFC is precisely "adjacency rules + AC-4 propagation + entropy-guided collapse." **Commonly skipped incorrectly:** thermal erosion. Developers implement hydraulic erosion and skip thermal because it seems redundant, but hydraulic alone produces V-shaped channels without the scree slopes and talus deposits that make terrain look weathered. The two complement each other and should be learned as a pair.

### Phase 4: Constraint-based and region-based systems

**Techniques:** WFC (simple tiled model), Voronoi/Delaunay partitioning, biome systems (Whittaker classification, scattered blend transitions), scalar field composition (multi-axis classification, smoothstep masking), river generation (Priority-Flood + D8 + flow accumulation), object placement pipeline (density fields, slope filtering, exclusion masks, hierarchical scattering).

This phase introduces techniques that require multiple inputs and produce cross-cutting outputs. WFC consumes adjacency rules and produces tile grids that satisfy global consistency. Voronoi consumes point sets and produces region partitions that feed biome assignment, adjacency graphs, and road networks. River generation consumes heightmaps and produces flow networks that constrain biome moisture, road routing, and settlement placement. The object placement pipeline consumes heightmaps, biome maps, and exclusion data to produce the final visible world content.

**Commonly learned too early:** overlapping-model WFC. The overlapping model is harder to control, slower, and less useful for game content than the simple tiled model. Developers should master simple tiled WFC and its constraint extensions (DeBroglie path connectivity, fixed tiles, boundary conditions) before exploring the overlapping model. **Commonly skipped incorrectly:** biome transition blending. Developers implement Whittaker classification and render hard biome boundaries, producing visually jarring results. KdotJPG's scattered blend is the correct solution but requires understanding normalized sparse convolution, which developers skip because it sounds mathematically intimidating despite being straightforward to implement.

### Phase 5: Architecture and world structure

**Techniques:** mission graphs (Dormans grammar), lock-and-key structures (Metazelda key-levels), graph grammars (context-sensitive subgraph rewriting), chunk-based streaming (coordinate-seeded generation, lazy loading), LOD-aware generation (CDLOD morphing, vertex skipping), boundary matching (height seams, normal seams, LOD cracks, content discontinuities), overworld graphs (region nodes, MST + reconnection), road networks (MST → A* → spline → terrain modification).

This phase orchestrates everything from earlier phases into coherent, streamable, structured worlds. It requires all prior techniques because overworld graphs reference biomes, dungeons, terrain, and placement systems. Chunk-based streaming requires coordinate-seeded noise, boundary matching requires extended grids and global normalization, and mission graphs require spatial realization from Phase 3 techniques.

**Commonly learned too early:** chunk-based generation. Developers implement chunking before they have a complete generation pipeline to chunk, creating architectural complexity without content to fill it. Chunking should be learned after the developer has a working non-chunked terrain + biome + placement pipeline and encounters the specific memory/streaming problem that chunking solves. **Commonly skipped incorrectly:** boundary matching. Developers implement chunks and accept visible seams, not realizing that height seams, normal seams, LOD cracks, and content discontinuities each require distinct solutions. Boundary matching is tedious but essential for professional quality.

## Part 5 — Open Questions and Documentation Gaps

The documentation landscape for procedural content generation is sharply bimodal: foundational techniques are exhaustively covered with interactive demos, reference implementations, and parameter guidance, while architectural and composition techniques are described conceptually but lack the implementation specifics needed to build them.

The best-documented techniques are noise primitives and their compositions. Quilez's iquilezles.org provides production-quality GLSL for fBm, domain warping, and SDF operations with spectral analysis and empirical validation. Gustavson's simplex noise tutorial remains the only source that fully explains the skewing transform with worked examples. Bridson's Poisson disk paper is a single page that completely specifies an O(N) algorithm. The Book of Shaders provides interactive editors for building intuition. For spatial generators, BSP dungeons (RogueBasin's JICE article), room-and-corridor (Nystrom's "Rooms and Mazes"), and CA caves (RogueBasin, Wolverson's Rust tutorial, Jeremy Kun's upscaling variant) all have multiple complete, tested implementations with parameter guidance. Autotiling is definitively covered by Boris the Brave's classification system and tileset taxonomy. WFC's simple tiled model has Boris the Brave's walkthrough, gridbugs.org's Rust implementation, and DeBroglie's constraint extension documentation. Hydraulic erosion has Lague's complete C#/compute shader with exact parameter defaults. Red Blob Games' polygon map generator provides a full pipeline from Voronoi seeds through biome assignment. These techniques can be implemented from published sources alone.

The adequately documented middle tier includes techniques where a determined developer can succeed but will encounter gaps requiring original engineering. Domain warping has Quilez's canonical examples but no systematic parameter guidance for game terrain specifically. Ridged multifractal has Musgrave's original C source but the inter-octave feedback mechanism is described more clearly in secondary sources than in the primary. WFC's overlapping model has Gumin's README and academic analysis but tuning the pattern size N and handling contradiction rates requires experimentation that no source shortcuts. Marching cubes has Bourke's lookup tables and Lewiner's MC33 ambiguity resolution, but integrating Transvoxel for LOD stitching requires combining two separate references. River generation on irregular meshes can be assembled from mapgen4's source code and Andrino's rivers-first article, but neither provides a self-contained tutorial.

The poorly documented tier contains critical techniques where implementation requires significant original engineering. Dormans' mission-to-space pipeline is the most consequential gap: his FDG 2010 and IEEE TCIAIG 2011 papers describe the grammar formalism and show example outputs, but provide no pseudocode for the spatial realization step, leave the "relative fitness" function for shape grammar rule application undefined, and give no parameters for the force-directed layout. A developer attempting to implement Dormans' pipeline from his papers alone will have to invent the spatial realization algorithm. Grammar authoring methodology is similarly undocumented: Unexplored's approximately 5,000 rules represent the only known production-scale grammar, and the systematic process for designing, testing, and balancing a grammar of that scale has never been published — Dormans acknowledges that grammar construction "may be relatively hard and time intensive" but provides no methodology beyond evolutionary search. Fungible key generation — producing levels solvable when keys are consumable and interchangeable — is explicitly identified as unsolved in the literature; no algorithm guarantees solvability with fungible keys.

Settlement and landmark placement exist at the conceptual level only. Cepero's pipeline and Patel's mapgen2-towns describe the scoring criteria (flatness, water access, separation) but publish no tested weight sets. Landmark placement — positioning visually distinctive features at navigationally significant locations — has zero algorithmic treatment in the accessible literature. Road network integration suffers from fragmentation: Galin et al.'s 2011 paper covers hierarchical cost-function routing, separate sources cover MST network construction and Catmull-Rom fitting, and the complete pipeline from settlement positions through spline fitting to terrain modification has never been documented in a single source. Dual contouring's QEF (quadratic error function) minimization has scattered implementation details across multiple sources, and manifold issues (non-manifold vertices producing holes in the mesh) are acknowledged but poorly addressed. Three-dimensional cellular automata for cave generation have no published standard parameters — the 2D rules (4-5 threshold, 15 iterations) do not transfer directly to 3D where the neighborhood size changes from 8 to 26. Chunk-boundary solutions for erosion, WFC, and biome blending each exist in isolation (erosion: global pre-pass at coarse resolution; WFC: Marian42's offset tiling; biome blending: KdotJPG's sparse convolution) but have never been combined or tested together in a documented system.

## Part 6 — Final Synthesis

### Best default approach lookup table

| Game Problem | Default Technique(s) | When to Deviate |
|---|---|---|
| Continents & coastlines | fBm + island mask + domain warping | Deviate to Voronoi dual mesh when designer needs explicit control over coastline shape or drainage network structure |
| Mountains | Ridged multifractal + cubed regional mask | Deviate to Voronoi F2−F1 ridge blending when mountain ranges must follow specific geographic lines rather than emerging from noise |
| Rivers | Priority-Flood + D8 + flow accumulation | Deviate to upstream-growth (mapgen4) when terrain is mesh-based rather than grid-based, or to rivers-first (Andrino) when river placement must be controlled before terrain exists |
| Caves | CA for 2D roguelike; dual-noise intersection for 3D streaming | Deviate to Perlin worms / agent tunneling when caves must follow designer-specified paths (connecting specific locations) or must have controlled width variation |
| Biomes | Whittaker lookup (temperature × moisture) + scattered blend | Deviate to Minecraft-style multi-noise (6D+) when more than two axes of variation are needed, or to local pre-computed assignment when neighbor constraints must prevent impossible biome adjacencies |
| Dungeons | BSP or room-and-corridor | Deviate to mission grammar + spatial realization when the game requires lock-and-key progression, critical path design, or Zelda-style item gating |
| Settlements | Multi-criteria terrain scoring + separation filter | Deviate to simulation-based growth when settlements must have emergent political territories or compete for resources — but expect to engineer the algorithm from scratch |
| Roads | MST of settlements + A* per edge on cost map + Catmull-Rom spline | Deviate to hierarchical network (Galin 2011) when the world has multiple scales of roads (highways, local roads, paths) that must form a coherent hierarchy |
| Vegetation | Hierarchical Poisson disk + density fields + slope/exclusion masks | Deviate to L-systems when individual plant geometry (branching trees, vines) must be generated, not just placement positions |
| World maps | Voronoi dual mesh + elevation + climate + biome pipeline | Deviate to pure noise-based approach when the world must be infinite/streaming and no pre-computed map can exist |
| Local area maps | Chunk-based fBm + LOD + boundary matching | Deviate to voxel terrain (3D density + marching cubes) when the game requires caves, overhangs, or destructible terrain as core mechanics |
| Progression spaces | Dormans-style mission grammar + lock-and-key | Deviate to space-first (BSP/CA) + post-hoc graph extraction when progression complexity is low (roguelike with minimal gating) or when development time prohibits grammar engineering |

### Most important techniques to learn first

The six highest-leverage foundation techniques are coordinate hashing, fBm, Poisson disk sampling, cellular automata, Voronoi/Delaunay partitioning, and WFC (simple tiled). This set is correct because each technique unlocks a distinct downstream capability that no other technique in the library provides. Coordinate hashing enables stateless deterministic randomness, which is prerequisite for chunk-based streaming, parallel generation, and reproducible worlds. fBm produces naturalistic multi-scale scalar fields, which are the input to terrain, biomes, density fields, cave volumes, and virtually every other spatial system. Poisson disk sampling produces blue-noise point distributions, which are the input to object placement, Voronoi seeding, and any system that needs spatially distributed candidates without clumping. Cellular automata teach the principle of emergent structure from local rules, which generalizes to understanding WFC, biome simulation, and agent-based systems. Voronoi/Delaunay partitioning produces spatial regions with natural adjacency, which is the foundation for biome systems, overworld graphs, road networks, and settlement territories. WFC demonstrates constraint propagation and global consistency maintenance, which is the most powerful principle for maintaining coherent output across large generated spaces. Together, these six techniques span the full range from noise evaluation through spatial partitioning to constraint satisfaction, and a developer who has implemented all six has the mental models needed to learn any remaining technique in the library within a few hours.

### Most important comparisons to understand

The three most consequential technique comparisons, ranked by the cost of choosing wrong, are: heightmap versus voxel terrain, space-first versus structure-first dungeons, and CA caves versus noise caves versus agent tunnels. The heightmap-versus-voxel decision is the most expensive to reverse because it determines the entire terrain data pipeline — mesh extraction, LOD, collision, physics, streaming architecture, and tool chain. A developer who builds a heightmap pipeline and later discovers the game needs caves or destructible terrain must rebuild from the storage layer up. The decision criterion is clear: if the game requires overhangs, caves as core content, or player-modified terrain, use voxels; otherwise, heightmaps are simpler, faster, and better documented. The space-first versus structure-first dungeon comparison is the second most consequential because it determines whether designed progression is possible. A developer who builds a BSP dungeon generator and later needs Zelda-style lock-and-key progression cannot retrofit structural intent onto space-first output — the architecture must be rebuilt around a mission graph. The criterion: if the game has keys, locks, required items, or designer-authored puzzle sequences, start with structure-first. The cave generation comparison (CA versus noise versus agents) matters because it determines chunk independence: noise-based caves are perfectly streamable, CA caves require knowing the full grid before connectivity can be verified, and agent tunnels require sequential state that prevents parallel chunk generation. A developer who chooses CA or agents for a streaming open world will discover at integration time that cave connectivity cannot be guaranteed per-chunk, requiring either a global pre-pass or tolerance of disconnected caverns.
